2.1
Metallic Bonding
▾

Definition

Metallic bonding is the strong electrostatic attraction between the nuclei of metal cations and the delocalised electrons surrounding them, extending throughout a giant lattice.

Delocalised electrons are outer-shell electrons that are not associated with any single atom or single bond — they are free to move throughout the whole structure (the "sea of electrons" model).

What affects the strength of metallic bonding

  • Number of delocalised electrons per atom (related to the charge on the cation) — more delocalised electrons means stronger attraction.
  • Size of the cation — a smaller cation lets the delocalised electrons get closer to the nuclei, increasing attraction.

This is why melting temperature and electrical conductivity increase in the order Na < Mg < Al: each step adds another delocalised electron per atom and the cation gets smaller, both strengthening the metallic bond.

Explaining metal properties from structure

  • Electrical conductivity — delocalised electrons are free to move towards the positive terminal when a potential difference is applied, carrying charge (works in both solid and molten states).
  • Thermal conductivity — free-moving delocalised electrons transfer kinetic energy through the structure, and the closely packed cations also pass on vibrational energy.
  • Malleability & ductility — when stress is applied, layers of cations can slide over one another; the delocalised electrons move with them, continuing to shield the cations from each other, so no strong repulsion builds up and the metal bends/reshapes rather than shattering.
Common slip: explaining malleability by saying "the ions can move" — be precise, it's the layers of cations sliding, with delocalised electrons moving to maintain the attraction as they do.
Definition
Define metallic bonding.
The strong electrostatic attraction between the nuclei of metal cations and the delocalised electrons surrounding them, throughout a giant lattice.
Explain
Why does melting point increase Na < Mg < Al?
Each element has more delocalised electrons per atom and a smaller cation size going Na→Mg→Al, both increasing the strength of metallic bonding.
Explain
Why are metals malleable?
Layers of cations can slide over each other under stress; delocalised electrons move with them, preventing strong repulsion between cation layers.
Explain
Why do metals conduct electricity?
Delocalised electrons are free to move through the structure and carry charge towards the positive terminal when a potential difference is applied.
2.2
Ionic Bonding
▾

Definition

Ionic bonding is the strong electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions, extending throughout a giant lattice in all directions (not a single "bond" between two atoms).

Formed by transfer of electrons: e.g. Na → Na⁺ + e⁻, and Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻. Shown using dot-and-cross diagrams to represent the electron transfer.

What affects the strength of ionic bonding

  • Ionic radius — smaller ions can get closer together, increasing the electrostatic attraction and strengthening the bond.
  • Ionic charge — greater charge on the ions increases the electrostatic attraction. E.g. CaO (2+/2−) has a much stronger ionic bond than NaCl (1+/1−).

Properties of ionic lattices

  • High melting/boiling points — the electrostatic forces extend throughout the giant lattice, so a large amount of energy is needed to break them all.
  • Conduct electricity only when molten or dissolved — ions must be free to move to carry charge; in the solid lattice, ions are fixed in position.
  • Often soluble in water — polar water molecules can surround and separate the ions (hydration).
  • Often brittle — if a layer shifts slightly, ions of the same charge can be brought next to each other, causing repulsion that shatters the lattice.
Definition
Define ionic bonding.
The strong electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions, extending throughout a giant lattice.
Compare
Why is the ionic bond in CaO stronger than in NaCl?
CaO has 2+ and 2− ions (compared to NaCl's 1+ and 1−), so the electrostatic attraction is greater — ionic charge has a big effect on bond strength.
Explain
Why can't solid ionic compounds conduct electricity?
The ions are fixed in position in the lattice and cannot move to carry charge; only when molten or dissolved are the ions free to move.
Explain
Why are ionic compounds often brittle?
A small shift of one layer can bring ions of the same charge next to each other; the resulting repulsion causes the lattice to shatter.
2.3
Covalent Bonding
▾

Formation & definition

A covalent bond forms when an atomic orbital containing a single electron on one atom overlaps with an atomic orbital containing a single electron on another atom, creating a shared pair of electrons between the two nuclei.

A covalent bond is the strong electrostatic attraction between the nuclei of two atoms and the shared pair of electrons between them.

Bond length vs bond strength

Shorter covalent bonds are stronger — the shared electron density is held more tightly between the two nuclei. Double and triple bonds between the same two atoms are shorter and stronger than the equivalent single bond.

Dot-and-cross diagrams

Represent shared electron pairs using a dot from one atom and a cross from the other. Used for discrete (simple) molecules, including those with multiple bonds and lone pairs, e.g. H₂O, NH₃, CH₄, O₂, N₂.

Definition
Define a covalent bond.
The strong electrostatic attraction between the nuclei of two atoms and a shared pair of electrons between them.
Compare
Which is stronger: a C=C double bond or a C–C single bond?
The C=C double bond — it is shorter and stronger than the single bond between the same two atoms.
2.4
Electronegativity & Bond Polarity
▾

Electronegativity

Electronegativity is the ability of an atom to attract the bonding pair of electrons in a covalent bond.

  • Increases across a period — more protons, similar shielding, smaller atomic radius, so a stronger pull on the bonding pair.
  • Decreases down a group — increased shielding and atomic radius outweigh the increased nuclear charge.
  • Fluorine is the most electronegative element.

Polar vs non-polar covalent bonds

When two bonded atoms have different electronegativities, the shared electron density sits unevenly between them, creating partial charges: δ+ on the less electronegative atom, δ− on the more electronegative atom. This is a polar covalent bond.

When the two atoms have the same (or very similar) electronegativity, electron density is shared symmetrically — a non-polar (pure) covalent bond.

Ionic and covalent bonding are two extremes of a continuum. A polar covalent bond can be thought of as having "some ionic character" — the more different the electronegativities, the more ionic the bond becomes.

Polar bonds vs polar molecules — don't confuse these!

Whether an overall molecule is polar depends on both (a) whether it has polar bonds, and (b) its shape/symmetry.

  • If a molecule is symmetrical, the individual bond dipoles can cancel out, giving a non-polar molecule overall — even though the bonds themselves are polar (e.g. CO₂, CCl₄, BF₃).
  • If the molecule is not symmetrical, or the dipoles don't fully cancel, the molecule is polar overall (e.g. H₂O, NH₃, CHCl₃).
This is a very common exam trap: "does molecule X have polar bonds?" and "is molecule X polar overall?" are two different questions — always check the shape before answering the second one.
Definition
Define electronegativity.
The ability of an atom to attract the bonding pair of electrons in a covalent bond.
Explain
Why does electronegativity increase across a period?
Nuclear charge increases while shielding stays roughly constant, and atomic radius decreases, so there's a stronger pull on the bonding electron pair.
Apply
CCl₄ has polar C–Cl bonds. Is the molecule polar overall? Why?
No — CCl₄ is tetrahedral and symmetrical, so the four bond dipoles cancel out, making the molecule non-polar overall.
Recall
Which element is the most electronegative?
Fluorine.
2.5
Dative Covalent Bonding
▾

Definition

A dative covalent (coordinate) bond is a covalent bond in which both electrons in the shared pair come from the same atom (the donor), rather than one from each atom.

Examples

  • NH₃ + H⁺ → NH₄⁺ — the lone pair on nitrogen forms the dative bond to H⁺.
  • NH₃ + BF₃ → NH₃BF₃ — the nitrogen lone pair is donated to boron.
  • AlCl₃ dimerisation to Al₂Cl₆ — a lone pair on a Cl atom of one AlCl₃ unit forms a dative bond to the Al of the other.
Once formed, a dative covalent bond is identical to a normal covalent bond in length and strength — only its origin (both electrons from one atom) is different. In displayed formulae, it's often shown with an arrow (→) from donor to acceptor.
Definition
Define a dative covalent bond.
A covalent bond in which both electrons of the shared pair come from the same atom (the donor).
Example
Which atom donates the lone pair when NH₃ reacts with H⁺ to form NH₄⁺?
Nitrogen — its lone pair forms the dative bond to H⁺.
2.6
Shapes of Molecules (Electron Pair Repulsion Theory)
▾

The basic idea

Electron pairs (bonding pairs and lone pairs) around a central atom repel each other and arrange themselves as far apart in space as possible, to minimise repulsion. This arrangement determines the molecule's 3D shape.

Repulsion strength order: lone pair–lone pair > lone pair–bond pair > bond pair–bond pair.

Each lone pair present slightly compresses the bond angles compared to the "ideal" shape, because lone pairs repel more strongly than bonding pairs.

A double or triple bond is treated as a single "electron group" for shape purposes — it still only counts as one region of electron density around the central atom.

Shapes and bond angles — the core set

Notice the trend: CH₄ (109.5°) → NH₃ (107°) → H₂O (104.5°). Each extra lone pair reduces the bond angle by roughly 2–3° due to stronger lone pair repulsion — a favourite examiner question.

Working out a shape from scratch

  1. Draw the dot-and-cross diagram to find the number of bonding pairs and lone pairs on the central atom.
  2. Count total electron pairs (treating multiple bonds as one group) to find the basic arrangement.
  3. Identify how many of those pairs are lone pairs, and apply the extra repulsion/angle compression.
  4. Name the shape based on the positions of the atoms only (lone pairs affect angles but aren't "seen" in the shape name).
Recall
State the repulsion strength order for electron pairs.
Lone pair–lone pair > lone pair–bond pair > bond pair–bond pair.
Shape
What shape and bond angle does NH₃ have?
Trigonal pyramidal, 107° (3 bond pairs, 1 lone pair on nitrogen).
Explain
Why is the bond angle in H₂O (104.5°) smaller than in NH₃ (107°)?
H₂O has two lone pairs (vs one in NH₃). Two lone pairs cause more repulsion on the bonding pairs than one, compressing the bond angle further.
Shape
What shape is PCl₅ and what are its bond angles?
Trigonal bipyramidal — 90° between axial and equatorial chlorines, 120° between the three equatorial chlorines.
Shape
What shape is CO₂ and why (despite carbon having 4 bonds)?
Linear, 180° — the two C=O double bonds each count as one electron group, giving 2 groups around carbon with no lone pairs.
2.7
Intermolecular Forces
▾

Three types (weakest concept to strongest, roughly)

  1. London (dispersion) forces
  2. Permanent dipole–dipole forces
  3. Hydrogen bonding

Van der Waals forces is the umbrella term for London forces + permanent dipole–dipole interactions combined.

London (dispersion) forces

Caused by the constant, random movement of electrons, which at any instant creates a temporary (instantaneous) dipole in a molecule. This instantaneous dipole induces a corresponding dipole in a neighbouring molecule, and the two attract.

  • Present between all molecules and atoms, whether polar or non-polar — it's the only intermolecular force between non-polar molecules (e.g. noble gases, I₂, CH₄).
  • Strength increases with more electrons in the molecule (larger molecular size/surface area) — this is why boiling points increase down Group 0 and Group 7.

Permanent dipole–dipole forces

Occur between molecules that have a permanent dipole (polar molecules whose bond dipoles don't cancel out). The δ+ end of one molecule is attracted to the δ− end of a neighbouring molecule.

This is an additional force on top of the London forces that are always present.

Hydrogen bonding

A particularly strong type of permanent dipole–dipole interaction. Occurs when a hydrogen atom is covalently bonded directly to a very small, highly electronegative atom — nitrogen, oxygen or fluorine (N, O, F) — which has a lone pair of electrons.

  • The δ+ hydrogen on one molecule is attracted to a lone pair on the N/O/F of a neighbouring molecule.
  • Any molecule with an O–H or N–H group can hydrogen bond (e.g. water, alcohols, ammonia, amines).
  • Hydrogen bonds are directional — the preferred bond angle is close to 180° (roughly linear), unlike the more random orientation of ordinary dipole–dipole attractions.
  • Hydrogen bonding is the strongest of the three intermolecular forces (though still much weaker than a covalent bond).
Recall
Name the three types of intermolecular force.
London (dispersion) forces, permanent dipole–dipole forces, and hydrogen bonding.
Explain
Why does boiling point increase down Group 7 (F₂ to I₂)?
Molecules have more electrons going down the group, increasing the strength of London forces between molecules, so more energy is needed to separate them.
Condition
What three conditions are needed for hydrogen bonding?
A hydrogen atom covalently bonded directly to a small, highly electronegative atom (N, O or F) that has a lone pair of electrons available.
Explain
Do non-polar molecules like CH₄ have any intermolecular forces?
Yes — London forces are present between all molecules regardless of polarity, caused by instantaneous dipoles from moving electrons.
2.8
Giant Structures & Properties Summary
▾

Giant covalent (macromolecular) structures

  • Diamond — each carbon atom is covalently bonded to 4 others in a tetrahedral arrangement, extending throughout the crystal. Extremely hard; very high melting point (many strong covalent bonds to break); does not conduct electricity (all electrons are held in localised covalent bonds, none delocalised).
  • Graphite — each carbon is covalently bonded to only 3 others, forming flat hexagonal layers. Within a layer, bonding is strong (giant covalent); between layers there are only weak London forces. The layers can slide over each other, making graphite soft and slippery (used as a lubricant). Each carbon has one electron not used in bonding, delocalised across each layer — this allows graphite to conduct electricity along the layers.
  • Graphene — a single isolated layer of graphite. Extremely strong (in-plane) and an excellent electrical conductor due to its delocalised electrons.

Common error: assuming all covalent structures have low melting points because "covalent = weak". Giant covalent structures (diamond, SiO₂) have very high melting points — it's simple molecular structures that are low-melting, because it's the weak intermolecular forces between molecules that break on melting, not the strong covalent bonds within them.
Explain
Why is graphite able to conduct electricity but diamond cannot?
In graphite, each carbon has one electron not used in bonding, which becomes delocalised across each layer and can carry charge. In diamond, all four outer electrons of each carbon are used in localised covalent bonds, so there are no free/delocalised electrons.
Explain
Why is graphite soft and slippery despite being a giant covalent structure?
The layers of graphite are held together only by weak London forces, so they can slide over each other easily, even though the covalent bonding within each layer is strong.
Explain
Why do simple molecular substances like I₂ have low melting points, despite having strong covalent bonds?
Melting only requires overcoming the weak intermolecular forces between molecules (London forces here) — the strong covalent bonds within each molecule are not broken.
