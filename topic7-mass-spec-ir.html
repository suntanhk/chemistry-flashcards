7.1a
Mass Spectrometry — Recap & Key Definitions
▾

Quick recap from Topic 1

Stages: Ionisation → Acceleration → Ion drift (flight tube) → Detection. The molecular ion (M⁺) forms by loss of one electron from the molecule, giving a peak whose m/z equals the relative molecular mass, Mr.

Key definitions for this topic

  • Molecular ion peak: the species formed from the molecule by the loss of one electron; its m/z gives the Mr of the compound.
  • Fragmentation: occurs when the molecular ion breaks apart into smaller pieces inside the mass spectrometer.
  • Base peak: the peak with the greatest abundance (tallest peak, set at 100% relative intensity) — represents the most stable fragment ion formed.
Don't confuse a peak's m/z value (which ion it is) with its height/abundance (how much of that ion is present) — they answer two completely different questions.

Reading a typical mass spectrum

Vertical axis: relative intensity (%), from 0–100. Horizontal axis: m/z (no units), usually from 0 up to just past the molecular ion peak.

Definition
Define the molecular ion peak.
The peak representing the species formed from the molecule by the loss of one electron; its m/z value equals the relative molecular mass of the compound.
Definition
Define the base peak.
The peak with the greatest abundance in the spectrum, representing the most stable fragment ion.
7.1b
Fragmentation & Deducing Structures
▾

What happens during fragmentation

When a carbon–carbon bond in the molecular ion breaks, it produces two species: a positive ion (which is detected) and a neutral species, usually a radical (which is not detected — mass spectrometers only detect charged species).

(CH3-CH3)+• → CH3+ + •CH3

Only positive ions produce peaks in the spectrum. If a bond breaks to form a positive ion and a radical, only the ion's m/z shows up — the radical is invisible to the detector.

Worked example — butane, CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₃ (Mr = 58)

m/zIon causing the peak
58Molecular ion, (C₄H₁₀)⁺•
43(CH₃CH₂CH₂)⁺ — loss of a methyl radical (•CH₃, mass 15) from the molecular ion
29(CH₃CH₂)⁺ — the ethyl cation
15(CH₃)⁺ — the methyl cation
Quick method: the "missing mass" between the molecular ion peak and a fragment peak tells you what was lost as a neutral radical. E.g. 58 − 43 = 15 → loss of •CH₃ (a methyl radical).

Common losses to recognise (missing mass → group lost)

Mass lostGroup lost
15•CH₃ (methyl radical)
17•OH (hydroxyl radical)
29•CHO or •C₂H₅
45•COOH (carboxyl radical)

Method for suggesting a structure from a mass spectrum

  1. Find the molecular ion peak (highest m/z) — this gives the Mr, and combined with a molecular formula, tells you what atoms are present.
  2. Work out the "missing mass" between the molecular ion and each fragment peak.
  3. Match each missing mass to a plausible lost group (see table above).
  4. Build up a structure consistent with all the fragment peaks given.
Explain
Why are radicals never seen as peaks in a mass spectrum?
Radicals are neutral (uncharged) species, and a mass spectrometer only detects and accelerates charged (ionised) particles — only positive ions produce peaks.
Apply
A compound has a molecular ion peak at m/z=72 and a fragment peak at m/z=57. What has been lost?
72 − 57 = 15, corresponding to loss of a methyl radical, •CH₃.
Recall
A fragment peak is 17 less than the molecular ion peak. What group has likely been lost?
•OH (a hydroxyl radical) — suggesting the original compound contains an –OH group, e.g. an alcohol.
7.2a
Infrared Spectroscopy — Theory
▾

What IR radiation does to a molecule

Infrared radiation is absorbed by covalent bonds, causing stretching (bond length increases and decreases) and bending vibrations. At this level, only stretching is considered.

The amount of energy (and so the frequency/wavenumber) absorbed by a bond depends on: the bond's length, the bond's strength, and the masses of the atoms involved.

Only bonds with a changing dipole absorb IR radiation — simple non-polar diatomic molecules like H₂ and Cl₂ do not absorb IR radiation at all, since there's no dipole to oscillate.

Reading the axes of an IR spectrum

  • Vertical axis: transmittance (%), from 0 to 100. 100% transmittance = no absorption at that wavenumber. A downward dip (trough) = an absorption.
  • Horizontal axis: wavenumber, in cm⁻¹ — the reciprocal of wavelength, so it represents frequency. Runs from 4000 cm⁻¹ down to 500 cm⁻¹ (numbers decrease left to right, but frequency increases left to right). The scale changes after 2000 cm⁻¹.

Sharp vs broad absorptions

Some absorptions are described as sharp (narrow wavenumber range) or broad (wide wavenumber range) — this shape is itself diagnostic. The O–H absorption in alcohols and carboxylic acids is characteristically broad due to hydrogen bonding, while most other absorptions (like C=O) are sharp.

Explain
Why do H₂ and Cl₂ not absorb infrared radiation?
They are simple non-polar molecules with no permanent dipole, so their bond vibrations don't involve a changing dipole for the IR radiation to interact with.
Recall
What does the vertical axis of an IR spectrum represent, and what does 100% mean?
Transmittance (%); 100% means all the infrared radiation at that wavenumber passed through the sample without being absorbed.
Explain
Why is the O–H absorption in alcohols typically broad rather than sharp?
Hydrogen bonding between O–H groups in neighbouring molecules varies the exact bond environment, spreading the absorption over a wider range of wavenumbers.

Interpreting Spectra — Wavenumber Table & Method
▾

Key wavenumber table (approximate ranges)

BondWavenumber / cm⁻¹Found in

These values don't need to be memorised precisely for the exam — a data sheet with this table is normally provided. What you must be able to do is USE the table to interpret a spectrum.

Method: interpreting an unknown spectrum

  1. Scan the spectrum for absorptions, and note their approximate wavenumber and whether they're broad or sharp.
  2. Match each absorption to the table to suggest which bond(s) could be responsible.
  3. Use absence of an absorption just as much as presence — ruling out a functional group can be just as informative as identifying one.

Worked example — distinguishing a ketone from a carboxylic acid

A spectrum shows a strong, sharp absorption at 1700 cm⁻¹ (consistent with C=O — could be a ketone OR a carboxylic acid) but shows no absorption in the 2500–3300 cm⁻¹ region.

Since carboxylic acids always show the broad O–H absorption in that region, its absence rules out a carboxylic acid — the compound must be a ketone (or another C=O-containing compound without an O–H group, e.g. an aldehyde or ester, which would need further evidence like the C–H aldehyde peak around 2700–2900 to fully confirm).
Recall
What wavenumber range is characteristic of a C=O bond, and what functional groups could it belong to?
1680–1750 cm⁻¹; could be an aldehyde, ketone, carboxylic acid or ester.
Distinguish
A spectrum has a C=O peak at 1700 cm⁻¹ but no broad absorption at 2500–3300 cm⁻¹. What can you conclude?
The compound is unlikely to be a carboxylic acid (which would show the broad O–H absorption in that region) — it's more likely a ketone or aldehyde.
Recall
What wavenumber range is characteristic of an O–H bond in an alcohol, and how does it look (sharp/broad)?
3230–3550 cm⁻¹, broad.
Method
Why is the ABSENCE of an absorption sometimes just as useful as its presence?
Ruling out a functional group (e.g. no O–H peak rules out alcohols/carboxylic acids) narrows down the possible structures just as effectively as confirming one that IS present.
