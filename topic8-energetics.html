8.1
Enthalpy, Exothermic & Endothermic Processes
▾

Key definitions

Enthalpy (H): a measure of the total energy of a system. You cannot measure enthalpy directly — only the enthalpy change (ΔH) during a process.

Enthalpy change: the heat energy transferred between the system and surroundings, measured at constant pressure.

  • Exothermic: heat energy is transferred from the system to the surroundings (ΔH is negative). Usually seen as a temperature increase.
  • Endothermic: heat energy is transferred from the surroundings to the system (ΔH is positive). Usually seen as a temperature decrease, or the need for continuous heating.
"System" = the reaction mixture itself. "Surroundings" = everything else (in practice, usually the air/water around the reaction vessel).

Enthalpy level diagrams

  • Vertical axis: enthalpy, H (labelled; the horizontal axis does not need a label, or can be "extent of reaction").
  • Exothermic: products drawn LOWER than reactants (H_products < H_reactants), arrow points down to products, ΔH negative.
  • Endothermic: products drawn HIGHER than reactants, arrow points up to products, ΔH positive.
  • Always include full formulae with state symbols for reactants/products, and the ΔH value with its correct sign.

ΔH = H(products) − H(reactants)

Definition
Define exothermic and endothermic.
Exothermic: heat energy is transferred from the system to the surroundings. Endothermic: heat energy is transferred from the surroundings to the system.
Explain
In an enthalpy level diagram, where are the products drawn for an exothermic reaction, and why?
Lower than the reactants — because H(products) is less than H(reactants), so ΔH = H(products) − H(reactants) is negative.
Apply
The thermal decomposition of calcium carbonate has ΔH = +178 kJ mol⁻¹. Is it exo- or endothermic, and what practical clue confirms this?
Endothermic (positive ΔH) — confirmed by the fact that it requires continuous heating to proceed, a typical sign of an endothermic reaction.
8.2
Standard Enthalpy Changes — Definitions
▾

Standard conditions

100 kPa pressure and a stated temperature (usually 298 K). Symbol: ΔH⦵ (or ΔH°).

Standard enthalpy change of reaction, ΔrH⦵

The enthalpy change when the reaction occurs in the molar quantities shown in the equation, under standard conditions. Important: "per mole" refers to one mole of the equation as written, not one mole of any particular reactant.

Standard enthalpy change of combustion, ΔcH⦵

The enthalpy change when one mole of a substance is completely burned in oxygen, under standard conditions.

Standard enthalpy change of formation, ΔfH⦵

The enthalpy change when one mole of a compound is formed from its elements in their standard states, under standard conditions.

C(s, graphite) + O2(g) → CO2(g) ΔfH⦵ = −394 kJ mol⁻¹

By definition, the standard enthalpy change of formation of any element in its standard state is always zero — you're "forming" the element from itself.

Standard enthalpy change of neutralisation, ΔneutH⦵

The enthalpy change when one mole of water is produced by the neutralisation of an acid with an alkali, under standard conditions.

For strong acid + strong alkali, the ionic equation is essentially always the same: H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H₂O(l) — this is why the standard enthalpy of neutralisation is remarkably constant (~−57 kJ mol⁻¹) across different strong acid/alkali combinations.

State symbols matter

ΔfH⦵ of liquid ethanol (−278 kJ mol⁻¹) is different from ΔfH⦵ of gaseous ethanol (−235 kJ mol⁻¹) — always check and state the correct physical state.

Definition
Define standard enthalpy change of formation.
The enthalpy change when one mole of a compound is formed from its elements in their standard states, under standard conditions (100 kPa, usually 298 K).
Recall
What is the standard enthalpy change of formation of an element in its standard state?
Zero, by definition.
Explain
Why is the standard enthalpy change of neutralisation so similar across different strong acid/alkali pairs?
Because strong acids and alkalis are fully ionised in solution, so the reaction is essentially always the same: H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H₂O(l).
8.3
Calorimetry — Experimental Determination
▾

Core equation

Q = mcΔT

Q = heat energy transferred (J), m = mass of water/solution heated (g), c = specific heat capacity (4.18 J g⁻¹ K⁻¹ for water/dilute aqueous solutions), ΔT = temperature change (K, numerically the same as °C).

Three-stage method (e.g. combustion of a liquid fuel)

  1. Stage 1: calculate Q = mcΔT using the mass and temperature change of the water heated.
  2. Stage 2: calculate n, the amount (mol) of fuel burned, using n = mass burned ÷ molar mass.
  3. Stage 3: calculate ΔH = −Q ÷ n (the negative sign reflects that heat leaving the fuel/system, Q, corresponds to an exothermic, negative ΔH for the fuel).
Sign check: if temperature increases, ΔT and Q are positive, but ΔH comes out negative — consistent with an exothermic reaction. If temperature decreases, ΔT and Q are negative, giving a positive ΔH — consistent with endothermic.

Worked example — combustion of ethanol

0.42 g of ethanol (M = 46.0) burned, heating 100.0 g of water by 24.5 °C.

Q = 100.0 × 4.18 × 24.5 = 10 241 J = 10.24 kJ n(ethanol) = 0.42 ÷ 46.0 = 9.13 × 10⁻³ mol ΔcH = −10.24 ÷ (9.13×10⁻³) = −1120 kJ mol⁻¹ (3 s.f.)

Sources of error / why experimental values differ from data-book values

  • Heat energy lost to the surrounding air, not all transferred to the water.
  • Heat energy transferred to the container (e.g. copper can) rather than the water.
  • Incomplete combustion (soot forming) means not all the fuel's energy is released as heat.
  • Non-standard conditions — e.g. water vapour rather than liquid water may be produced.
  • Evaporation of the fuel before/during combustion, so not all the "missing mass" was actually combusted.
Most of these errors mean the experimental (measured) value for an exothermic ΔH is LESS negative (a smaller magnitude) than the true/data-book value, because some heat never reaches the water being measured.

Neutralisation calorimetry — set-up specifics

Use an expanded polystyrene cup (good insulator, minimises heat loss) rather than a metal or glass container. Measure initial temperatures of acid and alkali separately, mix, stir, and record the maximum temperature reached. Assume the density and specific heat capacity of the mixed solution are the same as pure water (1 g cm⁻³ and 4.18 J g⁻¹ K⁻¹).

Formula
State the equation used to calculate heat energy transferred to water, with units.
Q = mcΔT, where m is in g, c = 4.18 J g⁻¹ K⁻¹, and ΔT is in K (or °C).
Explain
Why is the experimental enthalpy of combustion usually less exothermic (smaller magnitude) than the data-book value?
Heat is lost to the surrounding air and to the container itself, rather than all being transferred to the water, and combustion may be incomplete — all leading to an underestimate of the true heat released.
Explain
Why is an expanded polystyrene cup used for neutralisation calorimetry rather than a glass beaker?
Polystyrene is a much better thermal insulator, reducing heat loss to the surroundings and giving a more accurate maximum temperature reading.
8.4
Hess's Law & Enthalpy Cycles
▾

Hess's Law

Hess's Law states that the enthalpy change of a reaction is independent of the path taken in converting reactants into products, provided the initial and final conditions are the same. It's a consequence of the law of conservation of energy.

Hess's Law lets us calculate enthalpy changes that cannot be measured directly — e.g. the formation of CO(g) from carbon and oxygen can't be isolated experimentally (carbon always tends to burn to CO₂), so we use combustion data instead via a cycle.

General method for any Hess cycle

  1. Write the target equation (reactants → products) across the top of the cycle, with the unknown ΔH labelled.
  2. Choose a common set of species to route both reactants and products through — usually either their combustion products (CO₂ + H₂O) or their elements.
  3. Draw arrows from reactants down to this common set, and from products down to the same common set (or vice versa for elements).
  4. Follow the arrows: going the "same way" as the top arrow uses a + sign; going "against" an arrow uses a − sign.

Route via combustion data

Used when you're given ΔcH values for reactants and products (typical for calculating a ΔfH). Both reactants and products are routed DOWN to their combustion products, since burning is what the data represents.

ΔfH⦵ = Σ ΔcH⦵(elements) − ΔcH⦵(compound)

Worked example — formation of methanol, CH₃OH:

ΔcH[C(s)] = -394, ΔcH[H2(g)] = -286, ΔcH[CH3OH(l)] = -726 (all kJ/mol) ΔfH = (-394) + (2 × -286) - (-726) = -240 kJ mol⁻¹

Route via formation data

Used when you're given ΔfH values for everything in an equation (typical for calculating a ΔrH for some other reaction). Both reactants and products are routed UP from their elements, since formation data represents building compounds from elements.

ΔrH⦵ = Σ ΔfH⦵(products) − Σ ΔfH⦵(reactants)

Applying Hess's Law to any given data (general reactions)

Not every Hess cycle question uses combustion or formation data specifically — sometimes you're given a set of arbitrary reaction equations and enthalpies, and must add/subtract/reverse/scale them (like simultaneous equations) so they sum to the target equation. Reversing an equation flips the sign of its ΔH; multiplying an equation by a factor multiplies its ΔH by the same factor.

Statement
State Hess's Law.
The enthalpy change of a reaction is independent of the path taken in converting reactants into products, provided the initial and final conditions are the same.
Formula
Give the general formula for finding ΔfH from combustion data.
ΔfH⦵ = Σ ΔcH⦵(elements) − ΔcH⦵(compound)
Formula
Give the general formula for finding ΔrH from formation data.
ΔrH⦵ = Σ ΔfH⦵(products) − Σ ΔfH⦵(reactants)
Explain
Why can't the formation enthalpy of CO(g) be measured directly?
Burning carbon in oxygen cannot be controlled to stop cleanly at CO — it's very difficult to prevent some of it going on to form CO₂, so a Hess cycle using combustion data must be used instead.
Rule
If you reverse an equation to make it fit a Hess cycle, what happens to its ΔH value?
The sign of ΔH flips (positive becomes negative, or vice versa).
8.5
Bond Enthalpy & Mean Bond Enthalpy
▾

Definitions

Bond enthalpy: the enthalpy change when one mole of a specific bond, in the gaseous state, is broken.

Mean bond enthalpy, E(bond): the enthalpy change when one mole of a given bond, averaged out over many different molecules/environments, is broken.

The exact bond enthalpy of, say, a C–H bond varies slightly depending on which molecule it's in and which C–H bond in that molecule (successive bonds broken from the same molecule have different values) — the "mean" bond enthalpy averages over all these variations, which is why it gives only an approximate answer when used in calculations.

Bond breaking vs bond forming — sign convention

  • Breaking a bond always requires energy input — endothermic, positive.
  • Forming a bond always releases energy — exothermic, negative.

Calculating ΔrH from mean bond enthalpies

ΔrH = Σ E(bonds broken) − Σ E(bonds made)

Worked example: H₂(g) + Cl₂(g) → 2HCl(g), using E(H–H)=436, E(Cl–Cl)=244, E(H–Cl)=432 (kJ mol⁻¹)

Bonds broken: 436 + 244 = 680 Bonds made: 2 × 432 = 864 ΔrH = 680 − 864 = −184 kJ mol⁻¹

Tip for a molecule like H₂O₂ → H₂O + ½O₂: any bond that is IDENTICAL and present in both reactants and products (e.g. the two O–H bonds in H₂O₂, also present in H₂O) can simply be ignored/cancelled — you only need to consider the bonds that actually change.

Limitations of the mean bond enthalpy method

  • Mean bond enthalpies are averages across many compounds, so a calculated ΔH using them is only an estimate — it will usually differ from the experimentally measured value.
  • Mean bond enthalpy data applies to species in the gaseous state. If any reactant or product in the real reaction is actually a solid or liquid, the calculation ignores the extra enthalpy changes needed to convert between states (e.g. sublimation, vaporisation), introducing further error.
A very common exam answer that scores well: "the calculated value differs from the real value because mean bond enthalpies are average values taken across many different compounds, and/or because the reaction doesn't actually occur entirely in the gaseous state as the data assumes."
Definition
Define mean bond enthalpy.
The enthalpy change when one mole of a given bond, averaged over many different molecules, is broken (in the gaseous state).
Recall
Is bond breaking exothermic or endothermic? What about bond forming?
Bond breaking is always endothermic (requires energy input). Bond forming is always exothermic (releases energy).
Formula
State the equation for calculating ΔrH from mean bond enthalpies.
ΔrH = Σ E(bonds broken) − Σ E(bonds made)
Explain
Give two reasons why a ΔH calculated from mean bond enthalpies often differs from the experimental value.
1) Mean bond enthalpies are averaged across many different compounds, not specific to the exact molecule in the reaction. 2) The data assumes all species are gaseous, ignoring any additional enthalpy changes if a reactant/product is actually solid or liquid in reality.
