10.1
Reversible Reactions & Dynamic Equilibrium
▾

Conditions needed for dynamic equilibrium

  1. The reaction must be reversible.
  2. The reaction mixture must be in a closed container (nothing can enter or leave).

Definition — three key features

Dynamic equilibrium is a state in which:

  • Both the forward and backward reactions are continuously occurring (it isn't "stopped" — reactants and products keep interconverting).
  • The rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the backward reaction.
  • The concentrations of all reactants and products remain constant (but not necessarily equal to each other).
A very common wrong answer: describing equilibrium as a state where "the reaction has stopped" or "reactants = products in amount". Neither is correct — both reactions are still happening, and concentrations being constant does NOT mean they are equal to each other.
Recall
State the two conditions needed for dynamic equilibrium to be established.
The reaction must be reversible, and it must take place in a closed container.
Definition
State the three features of a system at dynamic equilibrium.
Both forward and backward reactions occur continuously; the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the backward reaction; the concentrations of reactants and products remain constant.
Careful!
Does "concentrations remain constant" mean reactant and product concentrations are equal at equilibrium?
No — the concentrations of reactants and products each stay constant over time, but they are not necessarily equal to one another.
10.2
Effect of Concentration & Pressure on Equilibrium Position
▾

Effect of concentration

Change madePosition of equilibrium shifts
Increase reactant concentrationTo the right
Decrease reactant concentrationTo the left
Increase product concentrationTo the left
Decrease product concentrationTo the right

Explanation: increasing a reactant's concentration increases the rate of the forward reaction; more product forms until the rate of the backward reaction increases enough to match it again, at a new equilibrium with slightly more product present.

Colour example: 2CrO₄²⁻(aq) [yellow] + 2H⁺(aq) ⇌ Cr₂O₇²⁻(aq) [orange] + H₂O(l). Adding acid (H⁺) shifts the position to the right (solution turns orange); adding alkali removes H⁺, shifting the position left (solution turns yellow).

Effect of pressure — gaseous equilibria only

Pressure only affects equilibria involving gases, and depends on the total number of gas moles on each side of the equation.

Moles of gas: reactants vs productsEffect of INCREASING pressure
More reactant moles than product molesShifts to the right (towards fewer moles)
Fewer reactant moles than product molesShifts to the left (towards fewer moles)
Equal moles on both sidesNo change in position

Rule of thumb: increasing pressure always shifts the equilibrium position towards the side with fewer gas moles (reduces the "crowding"); the reverse is true for a decrease in pressure.

Worked example: N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g) has 4 mol gas on the left, 2 mol gas on the right. Increasing pressure shifts the position to the right, increasing the percentage of ammonia in the equilibrium mixture — this is confirmed experimentally.
Apply
For 2SO₂(g) + O₂(g) ⇌ 2SO₃(g), what happens to the equilibrium position if pressure is increased?
Shifts to the right — there are 3 mol of gas on the left and 2 mol on the right, and increasing pressure favours the side with fewer gas moles.
Apply
For H₂(g) + I₂(g) ⇌ 2HI(g), what happens to the equilibrium position if pressure is increased?
No change — there are 2 mol of gas on both sides of the equation.
Explain
Explain why increasing the concentration of a reactant shifts the position of equilibrium to the right.
It increases the rate of the forward reaction; product concentration rises until the rate of the backward reaction increases enough to match it again, giving a new equilibrium with more product present.
10.3
Effect of Temperature & Catalysts
▾

Effect of temperature

Raising the temperature increases the rate of BOTH the forward and backward reactions, but increases the rate of the endothermic direction more than the exothermic direction. This shifts the equilibrium position towards the endothermic side.

Temperature changeThermicity of forward reactionPosition shifts
IncreasedExothermic (ΔH negative)To the left
DecreasedExothermic (ΔH negative)To the right
IncreasedEndothermic (ΔH positive)To the right
DecreasedEndothermic (ΔH positive)To the left
Precise wording matters: don't just say "temperature favours the endothermic direction" without explanation — both reactions speed up, but the endothermic one speeds up MORE, which is what actually shifts the position.

Effect of a catalyst on equilibrium

A catalyst increases the rate of both the forward and backward reactions by the same amount — so the position of equilibrium is NOT altered.

The benefit of a catalyst here is purely kinetic: it reduces the time needed to reach equilibrium, without changing the equilibrium composition itself.

Limitations of qualitative predictions

These concentration/pressure/temperature rules are useful predictions, but not full explanations, and can sometimes conflict — e.g. heating a sealed equilibrium mixture of brown NO₂(g) and colourless N₂O₄(g) raises BOTH temperature and pressure at once (since it's a fixed, closed container), and these two factors predict opposite shifts (exothermic forward reaction → temperature rise shifts left; fewer moles on the right → pressure rise shifts right). Only experimental observation (or calculation using the equilibrium constant) can resolve which effect wins in a specific case.

Apply
A forward reaction is exothermic. What happens to the equilibrium position if temperature is increased?
It shifts to the left (favouring the endothermic, backward reaction).
Careful!
Why is it inaccurate to say "increasing temperature favours the endothermic reaction and does nothing to the exothermic one"?
Increasing temperature increases the RATE of both the endothermic and exothermic reactions — it just increases the endothermic one's rate by more, which is what shifts the position.
Recall
Does adding a catalyst change the position of an equilibrium?
No — it speeds up the forward and backward reactions equally, reducing the time to reach equilibrium but not changing its composition.
10.4
The Equilibrium Constant, Kc
▾

The Equilibrium Law

For a general equilibrium aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD, the equilibrium constant is:

Kc = [C]ᶜ[D]ᵈ ÷ [A]ᵃ[B]ᵇ

Products (right-hand side) go in the numerator (top); reactants (left-hand side) go in the denominator (bottom) — always matching the equation as written, with each concentration raised to the power of its stoichiometric coefficient.

A Kc value is meaningless without stating which equation it refers to — the same reaction written with different coefficients (e.g. doubled) gives a different Kc value (specifically, squared), so always quote the equation alongside any Kc value.

Homogeneous vs heterogeneous equilibria

  • Homogeneous: all species are in the same phase (e.g. all gases, or all aqueous).
  • Heterogeneous: species are present in more than one phase (e.g. a solid in equilibrium with a gas).

Heterogeneous equilibria — omitting solids and pure liquids

The concentration of a pure solid or pure liquid is effectively constant (it doesn't change no matter how much is present, since concentration = mass ÷ volume, and both density and molar mass are fixed) — so these terms are left out of the Kc expression entirely (their constant value is absorbed into Kc itself).

CaCO3(s) ⇌ CaO(s) + CO2(g) Kc = [CO2(g)] (solids omitted)

Only aqueous species and gases appear as terms in a Kc expression — pure solids and pure liquids (including water as a solvent, or as a product/reactant in its pure liquid form) are omitted.

Using Kc to explain (not just predict) a shift in position

Kc has a fixed value at a given temperature. If a concentration is suddenly changed, the reaction quotient no longer equals Kc — the system must then shift in whichever direction restores the ratio to Kc's value.

Worked example: for 2NO₂(g) ⇌ N₂O₄(g), Kc = [N₂O₄] ÷ [NO₂]². If [NO₂] suddenly increases, the denominator increases, making the ratio momentarily less than Kc. To restore the ratio to Kc, [N₂O₄] must increase — meaning the position of equilibrium shifts to the right, until a new equilibrium is reached where the ratio again equals Kc.

This is the real explanation behind Le Chatelier-style predictions — Kc is what actually controls and explains the shift, whereas "position of equilibrium" language is just a convenient shorthand for describing the outcome.
Apply
Write the Kc expression for N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g).
Kc = [NH₃]² ÷ ([N₂][H₂]³)
Apply
Write the Kc expression for CaCO₃(s) ⇌ CaO(s) + CO₂(g).
Kc = [CO₂(g)] — the two solids, CaCO₃ and CaO, are omitted since their concentrations are constant.
Explain
Why are pure solids and pure liquids left out of a Kc expression?
Their concentration (mass ÷ volume) is fixed by their constant density and molar mass, regardless of how much is present — so this constant value is simply absorbed into Kc rather than written as a separate term.
Classify
Is PbCl₂(s) ⇌ Pb²⁺(aq) + 2Cl⁻(aq) a homogeneous or heterogeneous equilibrium?
Heterogeneous — it involves both a solid phase (PbCl₂) and an aqueous phase (the ions).
Explain
Using Kc, explain why increasing [NO₂] in 2NO₂(g) ⇌ N₂O₄(g) shifts the equilibrium to the right.
Kc = [N₂O₄]÷[NO₂]² is fixed at a given temperature. Increasing [NO₂] makes the ratio momentarily smaller than Kc; [N₂O₄] must then increase to restore the ratio to Kc, meaning the position shifts to the right.
