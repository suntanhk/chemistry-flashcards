5.1
The Mole, Avogadro Constant & Molar Mass
▾

Definitions

Mole: the amount of substance that contains the same number of particles as the number of carbon atoms in exactly 12 g of the carbon-12 isotope.

Avogadro constant (Lₐ or Nₐ): the number of atoms of ¹²C in exactly 12 g of ¹²C — approximately 6.02 × 10²³ mol⁻¹.

Molar mass (M): the mass per mole of a substance, units g mol⁻¹. Unlike "relative molecular mass" (which only applies to molecules), molar mass and "relative formula mass" can be used for ionic compounds too.

Core equation

n = m ÷ M

Rearranged forms: m = n × M and M = m ÷ n

Example: Amount of substance in 6.51 g of NaCl (M = 58.5): n = 6.51 ÷ 58.5 = 0.111 mol

Watch out for questions about "particles" (atoms, molecules or ions) — once you have moles, multiply by the Avogadro constant to get the actual number of particles: number of particles = n × Nₐ.
Definition
Define the mole.
The amount of substance that contains the same number of particles as the number of carbon atoms in exactly 12 g of carbon-12.
Calculate
What is the mass of 0.263 mol of hydrogen iodide, HI (M = 127.9)?
m = n × M = 0.263 × 127.9 = 33.6 g
Terminology
Why can't we say the "relative molecular mass" of NaOH is 40.0?
Relative molecular mass only applies to molecules (covalent compounds). NaOH is ionic, so we should use "relative formula mass" or "molar mass" instead.
5.2
Empirical & Molecular Formulae
▾

Empirical vs molecular formula

Empirical formula: the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms of each element in a compound.

Molecular formula: the actual number of atoms of each element in a molecule.

Example: hydrogen peroxide — empirical formula HO, molecular formula H₂O₂. Butane — empirical formula C₂H₅, molecular formula C₄H₁₀.

Finding molecular formula from empirical formula

You need the empirical formula AND the relative molecular mass (Mr). Compare Mr of the empirical formula to the actual Mr — the molecular formula is a whole-number multiple of the empirical formula.

Example: empirical formula is HO (Mr = 17); actual Mr = 34. Since 34 ÷ 17 = 2, the molecular formula is (HO)₂ = H₂O₂.

Determining Mr for a molecular formula calculation

Two common sources: mass spectrometry (the M peak gives Mr directly), or the ideal gas equation pV = nRT for a gas or volatile liquid (rearranged to find n, then M = m/n).

Definition
Define empirical formula.
The simplest whole-number ratio of atoms of each element present in a compound.
Apply
A compound has empirical formula CH₂ (Mr of empirical unit = 14) and actual Mr = 42. Find the molecular formula.
42 ÷ 14 = 3, so molecular formula is (CH₂)₃ = C₃H₆.
5.3
Writing & Balancing Equations
▾

Formulae to remember

Diatomic elements exist as molecules, not single atoms: O₂, H₂, N₂, Cl₂, Br₂, I₂ (not O, H, N, Cl...). Also remember: water = H₂O, sodium hydroxide = NaOH, nitric acid = HNO₃, iron(II) sulfate = FeSO₄, iron(III) oxide = Fe₂O₃, calcium carbonate = CaCO₃.

Balancing method

  1. Write the (unbalanced) formulae for reactants and products.
  2. Add coefficients (whole numbers, or fractions in organic chemistry) in front of species until each element has equal totals on both sides.

2C4H10 + 13O2 → 8CO2 + 10H2O

State symbols

(s) = solid, (l) = liquid, (g) = gas, (aq) = aqueous (dissolved in water).

A very common error: writing H₂O(aq) instead of H₂O(l) — water itself is never "(aq)"; that label is for substances dissolved IN water.

Ionic equations

Show only the species that actually change — remove "spectator ions" that appear unchanged on both sides.

Method: (1) write the full equation, (2) split soluble ionic compounds into their separate ions, (3) cross out anything appearing identically on both sides.

Full: NaOH(aq) + HNO3(aq) → NaNO3(aq) + H2O(l) Ionic: H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H2O(l)

Molecules and solids/precipitates are NOT split into ions — only aqueous ionic compounds are. In the precipitation example Pb²⁺ + SO₄²⁻ → PbSO₄(s), the solid product stays as a full formula because it's no longer free ions in solution.
Recall
Name the diatomic elements you must write as X₂, not X.
H₂, N₂, O₂, F₂, Cl₂, Br₂, I₂ (the classic "HOFBrINCl" list, minus astatine at this level).
Method
What are the three steps for writing a simplified ionic equation?
1) Write the full balanced equation. 2) Split soluble ionic compounds into separate ions. 3) Cancel any ions that appear identically on both sides (spectator ions).
Explain
Why is it possible to write a "convincing" ionic equation for two soluble salts mixing that actually represents no reaction?
If all ions cancel out as spectator ions, there is nothing left — this shows no reaction actually occurs, even though a full equation with state symbols might look plausible.
5.4
Reacting Mass Calculations
▾

Core idea

A balanced equation gives the mole ratio between any two species. Convert the given mass to moles, use the equation's ratio to find moles of the target species, then convert back to mass.

Step 1: n(given) = m ÷ M Step 2: use mole ratio from the equation to find n(target) Step 3: m(target) = n(target) × M(target)

Worked example

SO₃ + H₂O → H₂SO₄. What mass of SO₃ is needed to form 75.0 g of H₂SO₄?

n(H2SO4) = 75.0 ÷ 98.1 = 0.765 mol n(SO3) = 0.765 mol (1:1 ratio) m(SO3) = 0.765 × 80.1 = 61.3 g

Method
What are the three steps of a reacting-mass calculation?
1) Convert the given mass to moles (n=m/M). 2) Use the mole ratio from the balanced equation to find moles of the target species. 3) Convert moles of the target back to mass (m=n×M).
Apply
For N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃, how many moles of H₂ are needed to react with 1 mol of N₂?
3 moles, following the 1:3 ratio in the balanced equation.
5.5
Gas Volume Calculations
▾

Molar volume (Vm)

The volume occupied by 1 mole of any gas, at a given temperature and pressure. At RTP (room temperature and pressure, 298 K / 101 325 Pa):

Vm = 24.0 dm³ mol⁻¹ = 24 000 cm³ mol⁻¹

Vm = volume ÷ amount (mol) → amount = volume ÷ Vm → volume = amount × Vm

Always check whether the question wants dm³ or cm³ — mixing the two units (using 24.0 with a cm³ volume, or 24000 with a dm³ volume) is a very common error.

Ideal gas equation

Used for any gas (or volatile liquid above its boiling point) to find amount, and hence molar mass if the mass is also known:

pV = nRT

SI units are essential: p in Pa, V in m³, n in mol, R = 8.31 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹, T in K (°C + 273).

Avogadro's law

Equal volumes of different gases, at the same temperature and pressure, contain equal numbers of molecules. This means the volumes of gases that react together (or are produced) are in the same simple whole-number ratio as their mole ratio in the balanced equation, provided conditions are constant.

Example: N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃ means 1 volume of N₂ reacts with 3 volumes of H₂ to give 2 volumes of NH₃, at the same temperature and pressure.

Recall
State the molar volume of a gas at RTP.
24.0 dm³ mol⁻¹ (24 000 cm³ mol⁻¹).
Calculate
What volume (in dm³) does 0.356 mol of hydrogen occupy at RTP?
V = 24.0 × 0.356 = 8.54 dm³
Units
In the ideal gas equation pV=nRT, what SI units must p, V and T be in?
p in Pa, V in m³, T in Kelvin (with R = 8.31 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹).
State
State Avogadro's law.
Equal volumes of gases, measured at the same temperature and pressure, contain equal numbers of molecules.
5.6
Concentration, Standard Solutions & Titrations
▾

Concentration

c = n ÷ V

Concentration (mol dm⁻³) = amount (mol) ÷ volume (dm³). Remember: 1000 cm³ = 1 dm³, so a volume in cm³ must be divided by 1000 to convert to dm³ before use.

Standard solutions & primary standards

A standard solution is one whose concentration is accurately known. It's made from a primary standard — a substance that should be: high molar mass, high purity, chemically stable (doesn't decompose/react with air), doesn't absorb water from the air, soluble, and reacts rapidly/completely in titrations.

NaOH and HCl are NOT suitable primary standards — NaOH absorbs water and CO₂ from the air, and HCl only exists as a gas or already-dissolved solution (no solid form to weigh accurately).

Method: making a standard solution

  1. Weigh the primary standard accurately (weighing bottle, weighing by difference).
  2. Transfer to a beaker and dissolve completely in a small volume of deionised water.
  3. Transfer the solution to a volumetric flask via a funnel, rinsing the beaker/rod thoroughly and adding rinsings to the flask (this ensures none of the solid is lost).
  4. Make up to the graduation mark with deionised water, ensuring the bottom of the meniscus sits exactly on the line.
  5. Stopper and invert several times to mix uniformly.

Titration apparatus & method

Apparatus: conical flask, burette (usually 50 cm³), pipette (usually 25 cm³) + filler, funnel, white tile, indicator.

A titration measures the volume of one solution needed to exactly react with a known volume of another, allowing the unknown concentration to be calculated.

Formula
State the concentration equation and its units.
c = n ÷ V, where c is in mol dm⁻³, n in mol, V in dm³.
Explain
Why is sodium hydroxide not suitable as a primary standard?
It absorbs water vapour and carbon dioxide from the air, so an accurately weighed sample doesn't have a reliably known composition of pure NaOH.
Method
Why must you rinse the beaker and stirring rod into the volumetric flask when making a standard solution?
To ensure that all of the weighed solid is transferred into the flask — any left behind would make the true concentration lower than calculated.
5.7
Percentage Measurement Uncertainty
▾

Core formula

% uncertainty = (measurement uncertainty ÷ value recorded) × 100

Key rule: count the uncertainty once per reading taken

  • Burette: uncertainty ±0.05 cm³ per reading, but a titre needs TWO readings (start and end), so total = ±0.10 cm³.
  • Balance: if weighing "by difference" (two separate weighings, e.g. before and after), count the balance's uncertainty twice per weighing (once for taring/zeroing, once for the reading) and twice again if two weighings are taken — i.e. up to ×4 total for a "weigh, then re-weigh" method.
  • Pipette/volumetric flask: used once for a fixed volume, so the uncertainty is applied once.
Worked example: burette titre = 22.50 cm³, uncertainty = ±0.10 cm³ (two readings). % uncertainty = (0.10 ÷ 22.50) × 100 = 0.44%.

Minimising percentage uncertainty

  • Use larger measured values where possible — the same absolute uncertainty becomes a smaller percentage of a bigger reading (e.g. titre of 30 cm³ has lower % uncertainty than 10 cm³; weighing 5 g has lower % uncertainty than 0.5 g).
  • Total percentage uncertainty for a calculated result (e.g. concentration from a titration) = the SUM of the individual percentage uncertainties of every piece of apparatus used.
Rule
Why is a burette's total uncertainty in a titration ±0.10 cm³, not ±0.05 cm³?
A titre requires two readings (initial and final volume), so the ±0.05 cm³ uncertainty is counted twice, giving ±0.10 cm³ total.
Explain
Why does using a larger titre volume reduce percentage uncertainty?
The absolute uncertainty of the burette stays fixed (±0.10 cm³), but dividing this fixed amount by a larger titre volume gives a smaller percentage.
Method
How do you find the total percentage uncertainty in a value calculated from several measurements?
Add together the percentage uncertainties of every piece of apparatus used in the calculation.
5.8
Percentage Yield & Atom Economy
▾

Yield definitions

  • Theoretical yield: the maximum possible mass of product, calculated from the equation, assuming the reaction goes to completion with no losses.
  • Actual yield: the mass actually obtained by weighing in practice.
  • Percentage yield: (actual yield ÷ theoretical yield) × 100

Reasons actual yield is less than theoretical: the reaction may be reversible/incomplete, side reactions may form unwanted products, and purification steps can lose product.

Atom economy

atom economy = (molar mass of desired product ÷ sum of molar masses of ALL products) × 100

Developed by Barry Trost as a way to assess how "wasteful" a reaction is — a reaction with only one product automatically has 100% atom economy, since every atom from the reactants ends up in the desired product.

Atom economy and percentage yield are DIFFERENT and independent measures. A reaction could have 100% yield but low atom economy (if lots of atoms end up in an unwanted by-product), or vice versa. Both matter for evaluating a "green" industrial process.

Worked example — atom economy

Ca₃(PO₄)₂ + 3H₂SO₄ → 2H₃PO₄ + 3CaSO₄ (desired product: H₃PO₄, M=98.0; by-product CaSO₄, M=136.1)

atom economy = (98.0×2) ÷ [(98.0×2)+(136.1×3)] × 100 = 32.4%

Even with a 100% yield, less than a third of the reactant mass ends up as the desired product here — the rest becomes CaSO₄, which only "counts" economically if it can be sold or reused.

Reaction type and atom economy — a quick rule of thumb

Addition reactions (only one product, e.g. C₂H₄ + Cl₂ → C₂H₄Cl₂) always have 100% atom economy. Reactions that produce a second product (substitution, elimination with a small molecule leaving) will have atom economy below 100%.

Formula
State the percentage yield formula.
Percentage yield = (actual yield ÷ theoretical yield) × 100
Formula
State the atom economy formula.
Atom economy = (molar mass of desired product ÷ sum of molar masses of all products) × 100
Explain
Can a reaction have 100% percentage yield but low atom economy? Explain.
Yes — percentage yield only compares how much of the theoretically possible desired product was actually obtained, while atom economy measures what fraction of ALL the reactant atoms end up in the desired product versus by-products. They are independent measures.
Apply
Without calculating, explain why an addition reaction with only one product has 100% atom economy.
Since there is only one product, every atom from the reactants must end up in it — there's no by-product to "lose" mass to.
