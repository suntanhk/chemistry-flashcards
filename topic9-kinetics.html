9.1
Rate of Reaction & How to Measure It
▾

Definition

The rate of a chemical reaction is the change in concentration of a reactant or product per unit time.

rate = Δ[concentration] ÷ time

Units: if concentration is in mol dm⁻³ and time in seconds, rate is in mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹.

Reading rate from a graph

  • Concentration of reactant vs time: starts high, decreases, curve flattens to a gradient of zero once the reaction is complete.
  • Concentration/volume of product vs time: starts at zero, increases, levels off (flattens) once the reaction is complete.
  • The gradient of the curve at any point gives the rate of reaction at that instant. To find the rate at a specific point (not just the average over the whole reaction), draw a tangent to the curve at that point and calculate its gradient.
The steepest part of either curve is always at the very start (t=0) — this is the initial rate, since reactant concentration (and therefore collision frequency) is at its highest at the beginning.

Practical methods for measuring rate

  • Volume of gas produced over time (e.g. using a gas syringe) — rate = volume collected ÷ time taken.
  • Mass loss over time (e.g. CO₂ escaping from a reaction on a mass balance).
  • Colorimetry — tracking colour change/absorbance if a coloured species is a reactant or product.
  • Titration (sampling) — removing small samples at intervals and titrating to find remaining reactant concentration (usually with the reaction "quenched" first to stop it during the titration).
Definition
Define rate of reaction.
The change in concentration of a reactant or product per unit time.
Method
How do you find the rate of reaction at a specific point on a concentration-time curve (not just the average rate)?
Draw a tangent to the curve at that point, then calculate the gradient of the tangent.
Explain
Why is the rate of reaction fastest at the very start of a reaction?
Reactant concentration is at its highest at the start, giving the greatest frequency of collisions between reacting particles, so the gradient of the concentration-time graph is steepest there.
9.2
Collision Theory & Activation Energy
▾

The two requirements for a successful reaction

Particles must collide, but not every collision leads to a reaction. For a collision to be successful:

  1. The particles must collide with energy ≥ activation energy (Ea) — the minimum energy needed to start breaking bonds.
  2. The particles must collide with the correct orientation — the reactive parts of each molecule must meet.
Gas molecules collide extremely frequently (~10²⁸–10³⁰ collisions per second per dm³ at room temperature/pressure), yet most reactions take far longer than a fraction of a second — this is powerful evidence that only a tiny fraction of collisions are actually successful.

Activation energy — definition and role

Activation energy: the minimum energy required for a collision between particles to result in a reaction. Collisions with energy below Ea simply bounce apart with no reaction.

Every reaction involves some bond breaking (needs energy) before new bonds can form (releases energy) — activation energy corresponds to the energy needed to start this initial bond-breaking process.

Orientation — a worked example

In the reaction between ethene and HBr, only a collision where the H atom of H–Br approaches the C=C bond can lead to reaction — any other orientation (e.g. Br end approaching first) just results in the molecules bouncing apart, regardless of energy.

Collisions in solution vs gas phase

In solution, solute molecules are surrounded by solvent molecules ("trapped in a solvent cage"). Reaction in solution happens in two stages: (1) the molecules must first diffuse together into the same solvent cage, then (2) they must collide successfully within it. Despite this different picture, the same underlying kinetics apply — reaction rate is still proportional to the concentrations of the reacting species.

Definition
Define activation energy.
The minimum energy required for a collision between particles to result in a reaction.
Recall
State the two requirements for a successful collision.
The particles must collide with energy ≥ the activation energy, and with the correct orientation.
Explain
Given how frequently gas molecules collide, why don't gas-phase reactions finish almost instantly?
Only a very small fraction of the enormous number of collisions have both sufficient energy (≥ Ea) and the correct orientation to actually result in a reaction — most collisions are unsuccessful.
9.3
Maxwell–Boltzmann Distribution & Temperature
▾

Shape of the Maxwell–Boltzmann curve — four key features

  1. The curve is not symmetrical — it has a longer "tail" at higher energies.
  2. It starts at the origin (no molecules have zero energy) and approaches the x-axis asymptotically at high energy (never quite reaching zero — a few molecules always have very high energy).
  3. The area under the curve represents the total number of molecules, and stays constant regardless of temperature (since the number of molecules hasn't changed).
  4. At a higher temperature, the peak shifts to the right (higher average energy) and becomes lower/broader (same area spread over a wider energy range).
A very common exam mistake: drawing the higher-temperature curve with a taller peak. It must be LOWER and shifted right/broader than the lower-temperature curve — because the total area (total number of molecules) must stay the same.

Using the curve to explain the effect of temperature on rate

Mark the activation energy, Ea, as a vertical line on the graph. The area under the curve to the right of this line represents the fraction of molecules with energy ≥ Ea (i.e. able to react successfully on collision).

At a higher temperature, this shaded area (fraction of molecules with E ≥ Ea) is larger — so a greater fraction of collisions are successful, and the frequency of successful collisions per second increases. This is why increasing temperature increases the rate of reaction.

Full-mark answers must say the number/frequency of successful collisions increases — not just that "more collisions happen". The overall collision frequency does increase slightly with temperature too, but this effect is small compared to the much larger increase in the fraction of collisions exceeding Ea, which is the dominant reason for the large rate increase.
Recall
Why does the area under a Maxwell–Boltzmann curve stay the same at different temperatures?
The area represents the total number of molecules in the sample, which doesn't change with temperature.
Draw
How does the peak of the curve change when temperature increases?
It shifts to the right (higher energy) and becomes lower/broader — since the same total area is now spread across a wider range of higher energies.
Explain
Using the Maxwell–Boltzmann model, explain why increasing temperature increases reaction rate.
A greater fraction of molecules now have energy ≥ the activation energy (shown by the larger area under the curve to the right of the Ea line), so the frequency of successful collisions per second increases.
Careful!
What's wrong with the explanation "rate increases because there are more collisions at higher temperature"?
It's incomplete/misleading — the key reason is that more collisions are SUCCESSFUL (have energy ≥ Ea), not simply that there are more collisions overall. The increase in total collision frequency with temperature is a much smaller effect.
9.4
Catalysts
▾

How a catalyst works

A catalyst provides an alternative reaction route/mechanism with a lower activation energy. It is chemically unchanged at the end of the reaction.

Maxwell–Boltzmann diagram for a catalyst — do not confuse with the temperature diagram!

The catalyst version has ONE curve (temperature is unchanged) but TWO activation energy lines — one for the uncatalysed route (Ea) and one for the catalysed route (Ea, catalysed), which is further to the left (lower energy).

Since Ea(catalysed) is lower, a much larger area of the same curve now lies to the right of this new, lower Ea line — so a greater fraction of molecules have enough energy to react via the catalysed route, increasing the rate.

Exam trap: the temperature diagram has TWO curves and ONE Ea line; the catalyst diagram has ONE curve and TWO Ea lines. Mixing these two diagrams up is a very common error.

Reaction profile (enthalpy profile) diagrams

An extension of the enthalpy level diagram that also shows activation energy as a "hump" between reactants and products.

  • The catalysed pathway is drawn as a lower hump than the uncatalysed pathway.
  • Crucially, ΔH is the same for both the catalysed and uncatalysed routes — a catalyst changes the pathway (and thus Ea), but does NOT change the overall enthalpy change of the reaction.
  • For an endothermic reaction, the same idea applies, just with products drawn above reactants.

Homogeneous vs heterogeneous catalysts

  • Homogeneous catalyst: in the same phase as the reactants (e.g. a catalyst dissolved in the same solution as aqueous reactants).
  • Heterogeneous catalyst: in a different phase to the reactants (e.g. a solid catalyst used with gaseous reactants) — most industrial catalysts are heterogeneous. The reaction occurs at the catalyst's surface, so increasing the surface area (e.g. using a fine mesh, pellets or powder) increases the rate of the catalysed reaction.

Economic and environmental importance of catalysts

  • Lower activation energy means reactions can be run at a lower temperature, saving significant energy costs in industrial processes.
  • Catalysts are not used up, so (in principle) can be reused many times, unlike a reactant.
  • Using catalysts to allow lower-temperature/lower-energy routes reduces fuel consumption and associated CO₂ emissions — an environmental as well as economic benefit.
Explain
How does a catalyst increase the rate of a reaction?
It provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy, so a greater fraction of collisions have enough energy to react successfully.
Contrast
How many curves and how many activation energy lines are on the Maxwell–Boltzmann diagram showing the effect of a catalyst?
One curve (temperature unchanged) and two activation energy lines (one for the catalysed route, one for the uncatalysed route).
Recall
Does a catalyst change the overall enthalpy change (ΔH) of a reaction?
No — a catalyst only lowers the activation energy (changing the pathway); ΔH is the same with or without a catalyst.
Definition
Define a heterogeneous catalyst.
A catalyst that is in a different physical phase to the reactants (e.g. a solid catalyst with gaseous reactants).
Explain
Why does increasing the surface area of a heterogeneous catalyst increase the rate of reaction?
The reaction occurs at the catalyst's surface, so a greater surface area provides more sites for reactant molecules to adsorb and react, increasing the rate.
