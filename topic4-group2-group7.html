Group 2 — The Alkaline Earth Metals
4.1a
Trends: Ionisation Energy & Reactivity
▾

First and second ionisation energy

First IE: the energy required to remove one electron from each atom in one mole of gaseous atoms. M(g) → M⁺(g) + e⁻

Second IE: the energy required to remove one electron from each ion in one mole of gaseous 1+ ions. M⁺(g) → M²⁺(g) + e⁻

Group 2 elements always lose 2 electrons in reactions to form M²⁺ ions, so both first and second IE are relevant to their reactivity.

Trend down the group: both IEs decrease

Three factors are in play:

  • Nuclear charge increases down the group → would increase IE.
  • Atomic/ionic radius increases (extra quantum shells) → decreases IE.
  • Shielding from complete inner shells increases → decreases IE.

The combined effect of increasing radius and shielding outweighs the effect of increasing nuclear charge, so ionisation energy decreases down Group 2.

Trend in reactivity

Reactivity increases down Group 2. In every reaction, the Group 2 element forms an M²⁺ ion, and less energy is needed to remove the two electrons as you go down the group (lower combined 1st+2nd IE) — so the element reacts more readily.

Always link reactivity back to ionisation energy explicitly in your answer — "more reactive because it's lower down" with no reasoning given will not score full marks.
Definition
Define second ionisation energy.
The energy required to remove one electron from each ion in one mole of gaseous 1+ ions, forming one mole of gaseous 2+ ions.
Explain
Why does ionisation energy decrease down Group 2 despite increasing nuclear charge?
The increase in atomic radius and shielding from additional inner shells outweighs the increase in nuclear charge, so the outer electrons are less strongly held.
Explain
Why is barium more reactive than magnesium?
Barium has a lower combined first and second ionisation energy than magnesium (larger radius, more shielding), so its outer electrons are removed more easily, making it react more readily to form Ba²⁺.
4.1b
Reactions with Oxygen, Chlorine & Water
▾

With oxygen

2M(s) + O₂(g) → 2MO(s)

Forms a white oxide solid containing M²⁺ and O²⁻ ions. Reactions become more vigorous down the group. All Group 2 metals form a dull oxide surface coating even without heating, from slow reaction with air.

With chlorine

M(s) + Cl₂(g) → MCl₂(s)

Forms a white chloride solid containing M²⁺ and Cl⁻ ions. Also becomes more vigorous down the group.

With water

M(s) + 2H₂O(l) → M(OH)₂(aq/s) + H₂(g)

  • Magnesium reacts very slowly and incompletely with cold water.
  • Magnesium reacts vigorously with steam instead, forming MgO and H₂: Mg(s) + H₂O(g) → MgO(s) + H₂(g)
  • Calcium, strontium and barium react with increasing vigour down the group with cold water (seen as increasing effervescence).
  • Calcium hydroxide is only slightly soluble — the mixture goes cloudy as a precipitate forms: Ca(s) + 2H₂O(l) → Ca(OH)₂(s) + H₂(g)
  • Barium hydroxide is soluble, giving a colourless solution: Ba(s) + 2H₂O(l) → Ba(OH)₂(aq) + H₂(g)
Watch the state symbol for the hydroxide product — it changes from (s) for calcium (low solubility, precipitate forms) to (aq) for barium (soluble) — a common place to drop a mark.
Equation
Write the equation for magnesium reacting with steam.
Mg(s) + H₂O(g) → MgO(s) + H₂(g)
Observation
Why does the reaction of calcium with water go cloudy, but barium with water stays clear?
Calcium hydroxide has low solubility and forms a precipitate (cloudy), while barium hydroxide is soluble and dissolves fully (clear solution).
Recall
Write the general equation for a Group 2 metal reacting with chlorine.
M(s) + Cl₂(g) → MCl₂(s)
4.1c
Oxides, Hydroxides & Solubility Trends
▾

Oxides react with water to form alkaline hydroxides

MO(s) + H₂O(l) → M(OH)₂(aq)

Group 2 oxides are basic oxides. The ionic version, O²⁻ + H₂O → 2OH⁻, shows why the resulting solution is alkaline.

⚠️ Two opposite solubility trends — do not mix these up

  • Hydroxides: solubility INCREASES down the group. Mg(OH)₂ has very low solubility; Ba(OH)₂ is soluble. This means maximum alkalinity (pH) of the solution formed also increases down the group.
  • Sulfates: solubility DECREASES down the group. MgSO₄ is soluble; CaSO₄ is slightly soluble; SrSO₄ and BaSO₄ are insoluble.
This is one of the single most common Group 2 exam errors — hydroxide solubility goes up the group as you go down it, sulfate solubility goes down. Learn them as a contrasting pair, not in isolation.

Reactions with acids (neutralisation)

All Group 2 oxides and hydroxides react with acids to form a salt and water, e.g.:

MgO + H2SO4 → MgSO4 + H2O Ca(OH)2 + 2HNO3 → Ca(NO3)2 + 2H2O

Real-world applications

  • Limewater test for CO₂: CO₂ + Ca(OH)₂ → CaCO₃ + H₂O — white precipitate of insoluble calcium carbonate causes the "milky/cloudy" result.
  • Milk of magnesia: Mg(OH)₂ neutralises excess stomach HCl as an antacid: Mg(OH)₂ + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + 2H₂O. Safe because Mg(OH)₂'s low solubility keeps the OH⁻ concentration low.
  • Agricultural lime: Ca(OH)₂ neutralises excess acidity in soil.
  • Barium meal (medical imaging): BaSO₄ is used despite Ba²⁺ being toxic, because BaSO₄'s extremely low solubility means the ions are never free to be absorbed — it shows up soft tissue on X-rays.
  • Test for sulfate ions: add dilute acid (to remove interference from carbonate ions) then BaCl₂ or Ba(NO₃)₂ — a white precipitate of BaSO₄ confirms sulfate: Ba²⁺(aq) + SO₄²⁻(aq) → BaSO₄(s)
Trend
How does hydroxide solubility change down Group 2?
It increases down the group (Mg(OH)₂ almost insoluble → Ba(OH)₂ soluble).
Trend
How does sulfate solubility change down Group 2?
It decreases down the group (MgSO₄ soluble → BaSO₄ insoluble) — the opposite direction to hydroxides.
Explain
Why is barium sulfate safe to use in a barium meal despite Ba²⁺ being toxic?
BaSO₄ has extremely low solubility, so the Ba²⁺ ions are not free to be absorbed by the body — they pass through the digestive system without being released.
Test
Describe the test for sulfate ions in solution, including why acid is added first.
Add dilute acid (to remove carbonate ions, which would also give a false-positive white precipitate), then add barium chloride or barium nitrate solution — a white precipitate of BaSO₄ confirms sulfate ions are present.
4.1d
Thermal Stability of Nitrates & Carbonates
▾

Why Group 2 compounds decompose instead of just melting

Compared to a Group 1 chloride like NaCl (which simply melts with no decomposition), Group 2 nitrates and carbonates decompose on heating because:

  • The cation charge is higher (2+ vs 1+).
  • The cation is smaller (for elements in the same period).
  • The nitrate (NO₃⁻) and carbonate (CO₃²⁻) anions are large and more complex than a simple Cl⁻ ion.

A cation with a greater charge density (small size + high charge) distorts/polarises the large anion more strongly, making it easier for the anion to break down into a smaller, more stable ion (releasing a gas).

General trend across Group 1 and Group 2

Thermal stability increases down a group (bigger cation, lower charge density, less distortion of the anion) and is lower for Group 2 than Group 1 overall (higher charge, smaller radius for a given period).

Nitrates

All Group 2 nitrates undergo greater decomposition to the oxide, releasing brown NO₂ fumes and oxygen:

2M(NO₃)₂ → 2MO + 4NO₂ + O₂

Most Group 1 nitrates only undergo lesser decomposition to the nitrite (no brown fumes): 2NaNO₃ → 2NaNO₂ + O₂ — except lithium nitrate, which (like Group 2) decomposes fully to the oxide with brown fumes, because Li⁺ is unusually small for Group 1.

Carbonates

Group 2 carbonates decompose to the oxide and CO₂ (colourless gas, no visible fumes):

MCO₃ → MO + CO₂

Most Group 1 carbonates do not decompose when heated with a standard Bunsen flame (their thermal stability is much higher due to the 1+ charge) — again, lithium carbonate is the exception and does decompose.

Explain
Why do Group 2 nitrates/carbonates decompose on heating instead of just melting like NaCl?
Group 2 cations have a higher charge (2+) and smaller radius than Group 1 cations, giving them a higher charge density. This more strongly polarises/distorts the large nitrate or carbonate anion, making it break down into a smaller, more stable ion and release a gas.
Trend
Does thermal stability of Group 2 carbonates increase or decrease down the group?
Increases down the group — larger cations have lower charge density, so they distort the carbonate ion less, making it harder to decompose.
Exception
Which Group 1 nitrate behaves like a Group 2 nitrate on heating, and why?
Lithium nitrate — it decomposes fully to the oxide with brown NO₂ fumes, because Li⁺ is unusually small, giving it a higher charge density than the other Group 1 cations.
Equation
Write the general equation for thermal decomposition of a Group 2 carbonate.
MCO₃(s) → MO(s) + CO₂(g)
Group 7 — The Halogens
4.2a
Trends: Melting/Boiling Point & Electronegativity
▾

Melting and boiling point trend — increases down the group

Halogens exist as non-polar diatomic molecules (X₂). The only intermolecular force between them is London (dispersion) forces, arising from instantaneous dipole–induced dipole interactions.

Down the group, molecules have more electrons and a larger electron cloud, so London forces get stronger, requiring more energy to separate the molecules — hence increasing melting/boiling point.

This explains the change in physical state at room temperature: F₂ and Cl₂ are gases, Br₂ is a liquid, I₂ is a solid.

Electronegativity trend — decreases down the group

Electronegativity depends on nuclear charge, distance to the bonding pair (atomic radius), and shielding. Down Group 7, increasing atomic radius and shielding outweigh the increasing nuclear charge, so electronegativity decreases.

  • Group 7 has the highest electronegativity values of any group.
  • Fluorine is the most electronegative element of all.

Reactivity trend — decreases down the group

Halogens react mainly as oxidising agents, gaining electrons to form X⁻ ions. This ability is directly linked to electronegativity, so reactivity decreases down the group for the same reasons electronegativity decreases.

Explain
Why does boiling point increase down Group 7?
More electrons and a larger electron cloud down the group strengthen the London (instantaneous dipole–induced dipole) forces between molecules, needing more energy to separate them.
Recall
State the physical states of F₂, Cl₂, Br₂ and I₂ at room temperature.
F₂ and Cl₂: gases. Br₂: liquid. I₂: solid.
Explain
Why does reactivity decrease down Group 7?
Halogens react by gaining an electron (acting as oxidising agents); down the group, increasing atomic radius and shielding reduce the attraction for an incoming electron, so electronegativity — and reactivity — decrease.
4.2b
Redox Reactions with Metals & Halide Displacement
▾

Reactions with Group 1 and 2 metals

All involve electron transfer to the halogen — the halogen is reduced (oxidation number 0 → −1) and acts as the oxidising agent; the metal is oxidised (0 → +1 or +2).

2Li + Cl2 → 2LiCl Ba + Br2 → BaBr2

Most vigorous reaction: elements at the bottom of Groups 1/2 with elements at the top of Group 7 (e.g. caesium + fluorine).

Halogen/halide displacement reactions

A more reactive halogen displaces a less reactive halide from solution:

  • Chlorine displaces bromide and iodide.
  • Bromine displaces iodide, but NOT chloride.
  • Iodine cannot displace either chloride or bromide.

Cl2 + 2NaBr → 2NaCl + Br2 Br2 + 2NaI → 2NaBr + I2

These are redox reactions: the halogen's oxidation number decreases 0 → −1 (reduced), while the halide's oxidation number increases −1 → 0 (oxidised).

Explaining decreasing reactivity (Cl > Br > I)

  • Chlorine is the smallest atom, so an incoming electron gets closer to (and more attracted by) the nucleus.
  • Chlorine has the fewest complete inner shells, so an incoming electron experiences the least shielding/repulsion.

Observing the reactions — practical tip

Halogens dissolved in water can look similar in colour and vary with concentration, making results ambiguous. Adding an organic solvent (e.g. cyclohexane) and shaking makes the halogen dissolve preferentially into the upper organic layer, where its distinctive colour is much easier to identify (chlorine: very pale/colourless, bromine: orange, iodine: purple/violet).

Recall
Can bromine displace chlorine from a chloride solution?
No — bromine is less reactive than chlorine and can only displace less reactive halides (iodide), not more reactive ones (chloride).
Explain
Why is chlorine more reactive than bromine in displacement reactions?
Chlorine is a smaller atom with fewer complete inner shells, so an incoming electron is more strongly attracted to the nucleus and experiences less shielding, making chlorine gain an electron more readily.
Practical
Why is an organic solvent like cyclohexane added when observing halogen displacement reactions?
Halogens are more soluble in cyclohexane than water; shaking with it concentrates the halogen in the upper organic layer, making its characteristic colour much easier to identify than in the aqueous layer.
4.2c
Disproportionation Reactions of Chlorine
▾

Chlorine with water

Cl₂ + H₂O → HCl + HClO

Chlorine goes from 0 → −1 (reduced, in HCl) and 0 → +1 (oxidised, in HClO, chloric(I) acid/hypochlorous acid) simultaneously — a disproportionation reaction. This reaction is the basis for using chlorine to disinfect drinking water and swimming pools (kills pathogens).

Chlorine with cold dilute NaOH

Cl₂ + 2NaOH → NaCl + NaClO + H₂O

Chlorine: 0 → −1 (reduced, NaCl) and 0 → +1 (oxidised, NaClO, sodium chlorate(I)). Sodium chlorate(I) is the active bleaching ingredient in household bleach.

Chlorine with hot concentrated NaOH

3Cl₂ + 6NaOH → 5NaCl + NaClO₃ + 3H₂O

Chlorine: 0 → −1 (reduced, NaCl, ×5) and 0 → +5 (oxidised, NaClO₃, sodium chlorate(V), ×1). Sodium chlorate(V) is used in bleaching and as a weedkiller.

Note the different products depending on conditions: cold + dilute NaOH gives chlorate(I) (+1); hot + concentrated NaOH gives chlorate(V) (+5). Mixing these up is a very common error.
Equation
Write the equation for chlorine reacting with water, and name the products.
Cl₂ + H₂O → HCl + HClO. Products: hydrochloric acid and chloric(I) acid (hypochlorous acid).
Conditions
What product forms when chlorine reacts with cold dilute NaOH, vs hot concentrated NaOH?
Cold dilute: sodium chlorate(I), NaClO (Cl at +1). Hot concentrated: sodium chlorate(V), NaClO₃ (Cl at +5).
Explain
Why is the reaction of chlorine with NaOH classed as disproportionation?
Chlorine (oxidation number 0) is simultaneously reduced to −1 (in NaCl) and oxidised to +1 or +5 (in NaClO or NaClO₃) — the same element undergoes both oxidation and reduction in the same reaction.
4.2d
Testing for Halide Ions
▾

Method

Add dilute nitric acid first (removes carbonate ions, which would also give a false-positive precipitate), then add aqueous silver nitrate. A precipitate of the silver halide forms — its colour identifies the halide. Then add ammonia solution to distinguish between precipitates of similar colour.

Ag⁺(aq) + X⁻(aq) → AgX(s)

Results table

Halide+ AgNO₃+ dilute NH₃+ concentrated NH₃
Chloride (Cl⁻)White precipitateDissolves (soluble)Dissolves (soluble)
Bromide (Br⁻)Cream precipitateInsolubleDissolves (soluble)
Iodide (I⁻)Yellow precipitateInsolubleInsoluble
This test cannot be used for fluoride ions — silver fluoride is soluble in water, so no precipitate ever forms.
Recall
What colour precipitate does bromide give with silver nitrate?
Cream.
Explain
Why is dilute nitric acid added before the silver nitrate test?
To remove/prevent interference from carbonate ions, which would also form a precipitate with silver ions and give a false positive.
Distinguish
How does ammonia help distinguish between the three silver halide precipitates?
AgCl dissolves in dilute ammonia; AgBr is insoluble in dilute but dissolves in concentrated ammonia; AgI is insoluble in both dilute and concentrated ammonia.
Limitation
Why can't this test detect fluoride ions?
Silver fluoride is soluble in water, so no precipitate forms even when fluoride ions are present.
