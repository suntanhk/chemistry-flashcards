6.1 — Introduction to Organic Chemistry & Isomerism
6.1a
Representing & Naming Organic Molecules
▾

Key terms

  • Functional group: an atom or group of atoms that gives a molecule its characteristic chemical reactions.
  • Homologous series: a family of compounds with the same functional group and general formula, where each member differs from the next by CH₂, and which show a gradual change in physical properties (e.g. increasing boiling point) but similar chemical properties.
  • General formula: the simplest algebraic formula for a homologous series, e.g. alkanes CₙH₂ₙ₊₂, alcohols CₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH.

Ways of representing a molecule

  • Molecular formula: actual number of each atom, e.g. C₄H₁₀ (no structural detail).
  • Displayed (full structural) formula: shows every atom and every bond individually.
  • Structural formula: shows atoms grouped by carbon, e.g. CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₃ — enough detail to identify the structure without drawing every bond.
  • Skeletal formula: a zig-zag line showing only the carbon skeleton — each line-end/vertex represents a carbon atom, hydrogens are not shown, and only functional groups are drawn explicitly.

IUPAC nomenclature — key rules

  1. Find the longest continuous carbon chain that includes the principal (senior) functional group — this gives the stem name (meth-, eth-, prop-, but-, pent-...).
  2. Number the chain to give the lowest possible locants (position numbers) to the principal functional group first, then to substituents.
  3. Name and number all substituents/side chains, using di-, tri-, tetra- for repeats.
  4. List substituent prefixes in alphabetical order (not numerical), e.g. "4-chloro-3-methylhexane", not "3-methyl-4-chlorohexane" reversed.
  5. Use commas between numbers and hyphens between numbers and letters, e.g. "2,3-dichloro..."
Common error: numbering the chain to give the lowest locant to a substituent instead of the principal functional group — the functional group's position always takes priority when assigning the lowest number.
Definition
Define homologous series.
A family of compounds with the same functional group and general formula, where successive members differ by CH₂, showing a gradual change in physical properties but similar chemical properties.
Recall
What does a skeletal formula NOT show explicitly?
Hydrogen atoms and C–H bonds — only the carbon skeleton (as a zig-zag line) and functional groups are drawn.
Rule
In what order must multiple substituent prefixes be listed in an IUPAC name?
Alphabetical order, regardless of their position numbers.
6.1b
Isomerism
▾

Isomers — the overall definition

Isomers are molecules with the same molecular formula but a different arrangement of atoms in space.

Structural isomerism (three types)

  • Chain isomerism: different arrangement of the carbon skeleton (straight chain vs branched), e.g. butane and 2-methylpropane.
  • Positional isomerism: same carbon skeleton and same functional group, but the functional group is attached at a different position, e.g. butan-1-ol and butan-2-ol.
  • Functional group isomerism: same molecular formula but a completely different functional group, e.g. an alcohol (C₂H₆O, ethanol) and an ether (C₂H₆O, methoxymethane).

Stereoisomerism — E/Z (geometric) isomerism

Stereoisomers have the same structural formula but a different arrangement in space. E/Z isomerism arises because rotation around a C=C double bond is restricted (unlike a C–C single bond).

Conditions needed: (1) a C=C double bond restricting rotation, and (2) each carbon of the double bond must have two different groups attached.

  • Z-isomer ("zusammen", together): the two higher-priority groups (by atomic number, CIP priority rules) are on the same side of the double bond.
  • E-isomer ("entgegen", opposite): the two higher-priority groups are on opposite sides.
Cis-trans notation (an older, simpler system) only works when each carbon has one H and one other identical-type group — it breaks down when there are three or four different substituents on the double bond. E/Z notation, based on CIP priority, works for every case, so it's the system to default to.
Definition
Define isomers.
Molecules with the same molecular formula but a different arrangement of atoms in space.
Classify
Are propan-1-ol and propan-2-ol positional or chain isomers?
Positional isomers — same carbon skeleton and functional group, but the OH group is in a different position.
Condition
State the two conditions required for E/Z isomerism.
A C=C double bond (restricting rotation), and each carbon of the double bond must have two different groups attached.
Explain
Why does cis-trans notation sometimes fail, but E/Z notation always works?
Cis-trans only works cleanly when there's one hydrogen and one other group on each carbon of the double bond; with three or four different substituents, "same side as what?" becomes ambiguous. E/Z uses CIP priority rules to rank every substituent, so it always gives an unambiguous answer.
6.2 — Hydrocarbons
6.2a
Alkanes — Combustion & Free Radical Substitution
▾

Alkanes overview

Saturated hydrocarbons (only C–C and C–H single bonds), general formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₂. Relatively unreactive due to strong, non-polar C–C and C–H bonds.

Combustion

Complete combustion (excess oxygen): CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O

Incomplete combustion (limited oxygen) produces carbon monoxide and/or soot (carbon) instead of some or all of the CO₂ — a safety hazard in poorly ventilated gas appliances since CO is toxic and colourless/odourless.

Free radical substitution with halogens (e.g. CH₄ + Cl₂)

Requires UV light to start the reaction. Proceeds via three stages:

  1. Initiation: UV light provides enough energy to break the Cl–Cl bond by homolytic fission (each atom keeps one electron from the bonding pair), forming two chlorine radicals (Cl•).

    Cl2 → 2Cl•

  2. Propagation (two steps, repeated many times):

    Cl• + CH4 → •CH3 + HCl •CH3 + Cl2 → CH3Cl + Cl•

  3. Termination: two radicals combine to form a molecule, removing radicals from the system and ending the chain.

    Cl• + Cl• → Cl2 •CH3 + Cl• → CH3Cl •CH3 + •CH3 → C2H6

A radical (•) is a species with an unpaired electron. Homolytic fission is bond breaking where each atom takes one electron — this always produces two radicals, unlike heterolytic fission which produces ions.

Limitation of this reaction as a synthesis method: multiple substitution and substitution at different carbons produce a mixture of products, making it a poor method for making a single pure organic compound.

Definition
Define homolytic fission.
The breaking of a covalent bond so that each atom takes one electron from the bonding pair, forming two radicals.
Sequence
Name the three stages of free radical substitution, in order.
Initiation, propagation, termination.
Explain
Why is UV light needed to start the reaction between methane and chlorine?
UV light provides enough energy to break the Cl–Cl bond by homolytic fission, generating the chlorine radicals needed to start the chain reaction.
Limitation
Why is free radical substitution a poor method for preparing a single pure product?
Substitution can occur at any C–H bond and can happen more than once per molecule, producing a mixture of different substituted products that then need separating.
6.2b
Alkenes — Electrophilic Addition
▾

Alkenes overview

Unsaturated hydrocarbons containing at least one C=C double bond, general formula CₙH₂ₙ. More reactive than alkanes because the C=C double bond has a region of high electron density (the π bond) that attracts electrophiles.

Electrophilic addition — key definitions

Electrophile: an electron-pair acceptor — a species attracted to areas of high electron density.

Heterolytic fission: the breaking of a covalent bond so that BOTH electrons from the bonding pair go to one atom, forming two oppositely charged ions.

Electrophilic addition: a reaction in which two molecules combine to form a single product, and the attacking species is an electrophile.

Mechanism — addition of Br₂ to ethene

  1. The π bond electrons of the alkene are attracted to the slightly δ+ bromine atom of Br₂ (induced dipole, since the alkene's electron density polarises the approaching Br₂ molecule).
  2. A curly arrow goes from the C=C π bond to the nearer Br atom of Br₂; a second curly arrow goes from the Br–Br bond to the far bromine atom, causing heterolytic fission and forming Br⁻.
  3. This forms a positively charged intermediate — a carbocation — with the C now bonded to Br, plus a Br⁻ ion.
  4. The Br⁻ ion's lone pair then forms a new bond to the carbocation, with a curly arrow from the lone pair on Br⁻ to the positive carbon.
Mechanism-drawing marks depend on precision: curly arrows must start exactly from a bond or a lone pair (never from an atom or a "+" charge symbol) and point exactly to the atom/bond being formed.

Addition of hydrogen halides (e.g. HBr) — Markovnikov's rule

With an unsymmetrical alkene, a hydrogen halide can add in two possible orientations, forming different carbocation intermediates. The major product forms via the more stable carbocation.

Carbocation stability order: tertiary > secondary > primary — more alkyl groups attached to the positive carbon donate electron density (positive inductive effect), stabilising the charge.

Test for unsaturation

Shake the sample with orange bromine water. An alkene decolourises it rapidly (orange → colourless) via electrophilic addition, forming a colourless dibromo product. An alkane shows no colour change (no reaction) under normal conditions.

Definition
Define an electrophile.
An electron-pair acceptor, attracted to regions of high electron density.
Definition
Define heterolytic fission.
The breaking of a covalent bond so that both electrons of the bonding pair are taken by one of the two atoms, forming two oppositely charged ions.
Test
Describe the test to distinguish an alkene from an alkane.
Shake with orange bromine water. An alkene rapidly decolourises it (orange to colourless) via electrophilic addition; an alkane shows no colour change.
Explain
Why is a tertiary carbocation more stable than a primary one?
More alkyl groups are attached directly to the positively charged carbon; these donate electron density towards the positive charge (positive inductive effect), spreading out and stabilising the charge.
6.3 — Halogenoalkanes
6.3
Halogenoalkanes — Nucleophilic Substitution & Elimination
▾

Nucleophilic substitution — key definitions

Nucleophile: an electron-pair donor — attracted to regions of low electron density (positive/partially positive centres).

Nucleophilic substitution: a reaction in which an attacking nucleophile donates a lone pair to form a new bond, displacing another group (the halogen, as a halide ion, in halogenoalkanes).

Mechanism — hydrolysis of a halogenoalkane by OH⁻

  1. The carbon bonded to the halogen is δ+ (halogen is more electronegative), attracting the lone pair of the OH⁻ nucleophile.
  2. A curly arrow goes from a lone pair on the oxygen of OH⁻ to the δ+ carbon.
  3. Simultaneously, a curly arrow goes from the C–halogen bond to the halogen atom, forming a halide ion (heterolytic fission) as the halogen leaves.
  4. Product: an alcohol, plus a halide ion.

R–X + OH⁻ → R–OH + X⁻

Rate of hydrolysis — the C–X bond enthalpy trend

Rate of hydrolysis: R–I > R–Br > R–Cl > R–F (iodoalkanes react fastest, fluoroalkanes slowest/not at all under normal conditions).

The explanation is bond enthalpy, NOT electronegativity. C–I has the lowest bond enthalpy (weakest bond, longer/more diffuse orbital overlap going down the group), so it breaks most easily — even though iodine is the least electronegative of the halogens here. Electronegativity affects which atom becomes δ+/δ−, but bond strength (which is broken in the rate-determining step) is what controls the rate.

Elimination reactions

An elimination reaction is one in which a small molecule is removed from a larger molecule, without anything being substituted in its place, typically forming a C=C double bond.

Halogenoalkane + hot ethanolic KOH/NaOH (as a base, not a nucleophile here) → alkene + H₂O + halide ion (via loss of HX).

CH₃CH₂Br + OH⁻ → CH₂=CH₂ + H₂O + Br⁻

Same reagent (KOH), different conditions and different outcome: aqueous KOH/NaOH favours substitution (OH⁻ acts as a nucleophile, forming an alcohol); ethanolic KOH/NaOH favours elimination (OH⁻ acts as a base, forming an alkene). This contrast is a favourite exam question.
Definition
Define a nucleophile.
An electron-pair donor, attracted to regions of low electron density (positive or δ+ centres).
Explain
Why do iodoalkanes hydrolyse faster than chloroalkanes?
The C–I bond has a lower bond enthalpy (is weaker) than the C–Cl bond, so it breaks more easily — this is a bond enthalpy effect, not an electronegativity effect.
Contrast
What's the key difference between using aqueous KOH and ethanolic KOH with a halogenoalkane?
Aqueous KOH favours nucleophilic substitution (forms an alcohol); ethanolic KOH (usually with heat) favours elimination (forms an alkene), because OH⁻ acts as a base rather than a nucleophile in ethanol.
Definition
Define an elimination reaction.
A reaction in which a small molecule is removed from a larger molecule (without substitution), typically forming a double bond.
6.4 — Alcohols
6.4
Alcohols — Classification, Oxidation & Practical Techniques
▾

Overview

Alcohols are a homologous series with the –OH functional group, general formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH. They can hydrogen bond (via the O–H group), giving them higher boiling points and greater water solubility (for smaller alcohols) than alkanes of similar size.

Classifying alcohols

  • Primary (1°): the carbon bonded to –OH is attached to only one other alkyl group (or none, as in methanol).
  • Secondary (2°): the carbon bonded to –OH is attached to two other alkyl groups.
  • Tertiary (3°): the carbon bonded to –OH is attached to three other alkyl groups.

Oxidation of alcohols — the key A-grade distinction

Oxidising agent used: acidified potassium dichromate(VI), K₂Cr₂O₇/H⁺ — colour change orange → green as Cr(VI) is reduced to Cr(III).

  • Primary alcohols can be oxidised in two stages, controlled by technique:
    • Distillation (product removed as it forms, using limited oxidant) → stops at the aldehyde stage (partial oxidation).
    • Reflux (heating under a condenser that returns vapour to the flask, with excess oxidant) → continues to the carboxylic acid stage (complete oxidation).
  • Secondary alcohols are oxidised (under reflux) to ketones only — there is no further oxidation possible, since a ketone has no hydrogen left on the carbonyl carbon to remove.
  • Tertiary alcohols cannot be oxidised by acidified dichromate at all — there is no hydrogen atom on the carbon bonded to the –OH group for the oxidising agent to remove.
A very common exam trap: explaining why tertiary alcohols resist oxidation. The correct reason is structural (no H on the C–OH carbon to remove), not about "tertiary alcohols being unreactive" in general.

Distillation vs reflux — apparatus reasoning

TechniquePurposeKey apparatus feature
RefluxHeat a reaction mixture for a long time without losing volatile reactants/productsVertical condenser — vapour condenses and drips back into the flask
DistillationHeat and continuously remove/collect a volatile product as it formsCondenser angled downward — vapour is collected in a separate receiving flask

Other alcohol reactions

  • Combustion: alcohols burn in oxygen (often with a cleaner/less smoky flame than the equivalent hydrocarbon, since they already contain some oxygen).
  • Dehydration (elimination of water): heating an alcohol with an acid catalyst (e.g. concentrated H₃PO₄ or H₂SO₄) removes H₂O to form an alkene.
Classify
Is (CH₃)₃C-OH a primary, secondary or tertiary alcohol?
Tertiary — the carbon bonded to OH is attached to three other alkyl (methyl) groups.
Technique
Which technique — reflux or distillation — stops the oxidation of a primary alcohol at the aldehyde stage?
Distillation — the aldehyde is removed from the reaction mixture as soon as it forms, before it can be oxidised further to the carboxylic acid.
Explain
Why can't tertiary alcohols be oxidised by acidified dichromate?
There is no hydrogen atom attached to the carbon bearing the –OH group, so there is no hydrogen for the oxidising agent to remove.
Observation
Describe the colour change when a primary or secondary alcohol is oxidised by acidified potassium dichromate(VI).
Orange to green, as Cr(VI) (orange, in Cr₂O₇²⁻) is reduced to Cr(III) (green).
