Why This Guide Exists
▾

Passively reading a chapter top-to-bottom feels productive but produces weak recall. This guide turns the book's own built-in features into an active reading routine, so every hour with the book converts into exam-ready understanding — not just familiarity.

Page references and priority ratings here match the study plan and flashcard set exactly, so you can move between the book and the digital notes without losing your place.

2
Understanding the Book's Built-In Features
▾

Pearson built specific study tools into every section. Most students skim past them — used properly, each one has a precise job. Tap each card to see how to actually use that feature.

Topic opener
"What have I studied before? / What will I study later?"
Read this FIRST, every time. If a "studied before" point feels shaky, revisit it before starting the new content — new topics build directly on these.
Learning objectives
States exactly what you need to know by the end of the section.
Turn each objective into a question before reading (e.g. "Why does ionisation energy decrease down a group?"), then read to answer it, not just absorb text.
Key definitions
Bold, collated at the end of each section — the exact wording examiners expect.
Don't just recognise these. Cover the definition and recite it from memory. Precise wording is worth real marks at A level.
Worked examples
Shows the exact method and layout expected in your own answers.
Never just read one. Cover the solution, attempt it yourself on paper, then compare line by line.
Learning tips
Flags the most common mistakes students make.
Treat every Learning tip as a predicted exam trap — these are usually exactly where marks are lost.
Did you know?
Real-world facts featured to help memory.
Don't skip these as "just for fun" — a vivid real-world anchor is often what makes a definition stick under exam pressure.
In-text questions
Immediate self-check after each sub-section.
Answer every one before moving on. Answers are on Pearson's website (not printed) — look them up rather than skipping the check.
Thinking Bigger
Real research/writing extension spreads at the end of each topic.
Lower priority for resit prep. Useful for the highest-level exam questions, but only tackle these once the core content checklist is done.
Exam-style questions
Practice questions at the end of every topic.
Do these under timed conditions, then mark against the worked-example standard of precision shown earlier in the chapter — your most valuable practice time.
ActiveBook
Your 3-year online access — highlighting and annotation tools.
Use highlighting ONLY for key definitions and Learning tips — highlighting everything defeats the purpose. Use annotations to link a section to its matching flashcard page.
3
The Active Reading Protocol
▾

Apply this exact sequence to every section of every chapter. It takes slightly longer than passive reading the first time, but drastically cuts revision time later, because the material is properly understood the first time through.

Read the topic opener. Confirm you're solid on every "studied before" point.
Turn each Learning objective into a question. Write these questions down.
Read the main content once, without stopping to take notes.
Re-read slowly. Stop at every worked example and attempt it yourself before reading the solution.
Recite each Key definition from memory, then check the exact wording.
Answer the in-text questions, checking answers via Pearson's website.
Open the matching flashcard page (Section 4 below) and work through the flashcards for that section — this checks recall in a different format, catching gaps that re-reading alone misses.
Tick the matching checklist item on the flashcard page once you can answer it confidently without notes.
Steps 1–3 build a first-pass mental map. Step 4 is where real learning happens — attempting a worked example yourself exposes exactly which part of the method you don't yet have. Steps 6–8 test recall in two different formats, which is far more reliable evidence of true understanding than "I read the chapter" alone.

Reading focus per chapter

  • Topic 1: Work every worked example on electron configuration by hand before checking it — fastest way to catch the Cr/Cu exception errors.
  • Topic 2: Redraw every VSEPR shape without looking, then check. Watch the polar bond vs polar molecule distinction.
  • Topic 3: Practise the oxidation-number worked examples on paper, not just reading them.
  • Topic 4.1: Build your own two-column table of hydroxide vs sulfate solubility as you read.
  • Topic 4.2: Focus on halogen displacement and disproportionation worked examples.
  • Topic 5: Rewards slow reading — copy out every worked example step by step, including units.
  • Topic 6.1: Use the IUPAC naming examples to practise naming 5 molecules of your own, then check.
  • Topic 6.2: Redraw the electrophilic addition and free radical substitution mechanisms from memory each time.
  • Topic 6.3: Note how the book links rate of hydrolysis to bond enthalpy data, not just electronegativity.
  • Topic 6.4: Sketch your own distillation vs reflux apparatus diagram alongside the book's.
  • Topic 7: Practise the fragmentation "missing mass" method on every example; keep the wavenumber table handy.
  • Topic 8: Draw every Hess cycle yourself on paper before looking at the book's version.
  • Topic 9: Redraw the Maxwell–Boltzmann diagrams for temperature AND catalysts separately, to avoid mixing them up.
  • Topic 10: Write out the Kc expression for every example given, including heterogeneous ones, before checking.
5
Handling the Parts the Book Doesn't Cover
▾

Student Book 1 does not include NMR spectroscopy, chromatography, or chirality/optical isomerism — these Summer 2 topics belong to Book 2. Since there's no textbook chapter to apply the reading protocol to, use the school's own Summer 2 class notes instead, and still work through the matching outline in the study plan's Summer 2 Gaps section.
