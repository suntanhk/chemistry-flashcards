Fundamental Particles & Isotopes
▾

Sub-atomic particles

ParticleRelative massRelative chargeLocation
Proton1+1Nucleus
Neutron10Nucleus
Electron1/1840 (≈0.0005)−1Shells around nucleus

Learning tip: the electron's mass is so small it's usually treated as negligible when calculating the mass of an atom — but never treat its charge as negligible.

Key numbers

  • Atomic number (Z) = number of protons = number of electrons in a neutral atom. Defines the element.
  • Mass number (A) = number of protons + number of neutrons.
  • Number of neutrons = A − Z.

Isotopes

Isotopes are atoms of the same element (same number of protons/atomic number) with a different number of neutrons (different mass number).

  • Same chemical properties — chemical behaviour is governed by electron configuration, which is identical between isotopes.
  • Slightly different physical properties that depend on mass — e.g. density, rate of diffusion, rate of effusion.
Common slip: don't say isotopes have "the same properties" without qualifying chemical vs physical — examiners want the distinction stated explicitly.

Relative atomic mass (Ar)

Definition: the weighted mean mass of an atom of an element, relative to 1/12th the mass of an atom of carbon-12, taking into account the relative abundances of its isotopes.

A_r = Σ(isotopic mass × % abundance) ÷ 100

Worked example: Chlorine is 75% ³⁵Cl and 25% ³⁷Cl.
A_r = (35 × 75 + 37 × 25) ÷ 100 = (2625 + 925) ÷ 100 = 35.5

Definition
Define relative atomic mass, Ar.
The weighted mean mass of an atom of an element relative to 1/12 the mass of a carbon-12 atom, taking isotopic abundance into account.
Definition
Define isotopes.
Atoms of the same element (same number of protons) with different numbers of neutrons (different mass numbers).
Recall
Relative mass and charge of a proton, neutron, electron?
Proton: 1, +1. Neutron: 1, 0. Electron: ≈1/1840, −1.
Calculation
Bromine is 50% ⁷⁹Br and 50% ⁸¹Br. Find Ar.
(79 × 50 + 81 × 50) ÷ 100 = 80
1.2
Mass Spectrometry (Time-of-Flight)
▾

The four stages — in order

  • 1. Ionisation — the sample is vaporised then ionised, most commonly by:
    • Electrospray ionisation: sample dissolved in a volatile solvent, pushed through a fine needle at high voltage; each molecule gains a proton (H⁺) to form a molecular ion, M + H⁺ → MH⁺
    • Electron impact ionisation: sample vaporised, bombarded with high-energy electrons, knocking out an electron to form a positive ion (X → X⁺ + e⁻); can also cause fragmentation
  • 2. Acceleration — positive ions are accelerated by an electric field so that all ions leaving this stage have the same kinetic energy.
  • 3. Ion drift (flight tube) — ions travel down a tube with no electric/magnetic field. Because KE is equal for all ions, ½mv² = constant, so lighter ions (smaller m/z) travel faster and arrive first.
  • 4. Detection — ions hit a detector, are neutralised by gaining an electron, and the resulting current is proportional to the abundance of ions arriving. This builds the mass spectrum (m/z on x-axis, relative abundance on y-axis).

Reading the spectrum

  • Each peak's m/z corresponds to the mass of an isotope/fragment (since most ions formed are singly charged, m/z ≈ mass).
  • Peak height/intensity = relative abundance of that isotope or fragment.
  • Molecular ion peak (M peak) = the peak with the highest m/z value (aside from minor isotope peaks) — gives the relative molecular mass, Mr.
  • M+1 peak: a small peak one mass unit higher than the M peak, caused by the natural ~1.1% abundance of carbon-13 in organic molecules. Becomes more significant (relatively taller) in larger molecules with more carbon atoms.

Calculating Ar or Mr from spectrum data

A_r = Σ(m/z of isotope × relative abundance) ÷ Σ(relative abundances)

For compounds, you're usually just asked to add up the A_r values of all atoms present — full isotopic-composition calculations for molecules (like Cl₂) are only needed for the trickier questions.
Sequence
State the 4 stages of TOF mass spectrometry in order.
Ionisation → Acceleration → Ion drift (flight tube) → Detection
Explain
Why do lighter ions reach the detector faster?
All ions are given the same kinetic energy during acceleration. Since KE=½mv², for a fixed KE, smaller mass (m) means larger velocity (v), so lighter ions travel faster through the flight tube.
Explain
What causes the M+1 peak in an organic mass spectrum?
The natural presence of a small percentage of carbon-13 isotope in the molecule, adding 1 mass unit.
Recall
Name two methods of ionisation used in mass spectrometry.
Electrospray ionisation and electron impact ionisation.

Electron Structure: Shells, Subshells & Orbitals
▾

Shells

Electrons occupy quantum shells (principal quantum number, n = 1, 2, 3…) at increasing distance and energy from the nucleus. Maximum electrons in shell n = 2n².

Subshells

Each shell (except shell 1) splits into subshells of slightly different energy, labelled s, p, d, f.

Shell (n)Subshells present
11s
22s, 2p
33s, 3p, 3d
44s, 4p, 4d, 4f

Max electrons per subshell: s = 2, p = 6, d = 10, f = 14.

Orbitals

An orbital is a region of space with a 90% (or greater) probability of containing an electron. Each orbital holds a maximum of 2 electrons, which must have opposite spins.

  • s orbital — spherical; 1 per s subshell.
  • p orbital — dumbbell-shaped; 3 per p subshell (px, py, pz), oriented at right angles to each other.
  • d orbital — 5 per d subshell; shapes not required at this level.
Hund's rule: electrons fill separate orbitals within a subshell singly (same spin) before any pairing occurs — minimises electron-electron repulsion.

Writing electron configurations

Filling order (Aufbau principle, lowest energy first):

1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d → 4p → 5s → 4d…

Example — sodium (Z=11): 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s¹, shorthand [Ne] 3s¹

Key exception #1 — 4s fills before 3d (4s is lower energy in the neutral atom) but empties first when forming a positive ion (once occupied, 3d drops below 4s in energy). E.g. Fe is [Ar]3d⁶4s², but Fe²⁺ is [Ar]3d⁶ (not 3d⁴4s²).
Key exception #2 — Chromium and Copper: Cr is [Ar]3d⁵4s¹ (not 3d⁴4s²) and Cu is [Ar]3d¹⁰4s¹ (not 3d⁹4s²). A half-filled or fully-filled d subshell gives extra stability, so one 4s electron moves into the 3d subshell.
Recall
Max electrons in s, p and d subshells?
s = 2, p = 6, d = 10
Exception
Write the electron configuration of chromium (Z=24) and explain the anomaly.
[Ar]3d⁵4s¹ (not 3d⁴4s²) — a half-filled d subshell (5 unpaired electrons) is more stable, so one 4s electron shifts into 3d.
Definition
Define an atomic orbital.
A region of space around the nucleus with a 90% (or greater) probability of containing an electron, holding a maximum of 2 electrons with opposite spins.
Ion
Give the electron configuration of Fe²⁺ (Fe, Z=26).
[Ar]3d⁶ — the two electrons lost come from 4s, not 3d, since 3d is lower energy once occupied.
Rule
State Hund's rule.
Electrons occupy separate orbitals of the same subshell singly, with parallel spin, before any pairing occurs.
Shape
How many p orbitals are there per p subshell, and what shape are they?
Three (p_x, p_y, p_z), each dumbbell-shaped, oriented at right angles to each other.
1.4
Ionisation Energy
▾

Definition — first ionisation energy

The energy required to remove one electron from each atom in one mole of gaseous atoms, to form one mole of gaseous 1+ ions.

X(g) → X⁺(g) + e⁻

Every word in the definition is markable: "one electron", "each atom", "one mole", "gaseous atoms" (not solid/liquid), "gaseous 1+ ions". Missing "gaseous" is one of the most common lost marks.

Three factors that affect ionisation energy

  • Nuclear charge — more protons → stronger attraction on outer electrons → higher IE.
  • Atomic radius (distance) — electron further from the nucleus → weaker attraction → lower IE.
  • Shielding — inner shell electrons repel/shield outer electrons from the full nuclear charge; more inner shells → more shielding → lower IE.
Full-mark explanations must weigh up which factor dominates, not just list all three. E.g. down a group, radius and shielding effects outweigh the increase in nuclear charge.

Trend across a period (left to right)

Generally increases: nuclear charge increases, shielding stays roughly constant (same shell), atomic radius decreases — so attraction on outer electron gets stronger.

Two anomalies (dips), e.g. across Period 2/3:

  • Group 2 → Group 3 dip (e.g. Mg→Al, Be→B): the outer electron in Group 3 is in a p subshell, which is higher energy and slightly further out/more shielded than the s subshell — easier to remove.
  • Group 5 → Group 6 dip (e.g. P→S, N→O): Group 6 has its first paired p electron. Electron-electron repulsion in the paired orbital makes that electron easier to remove than a lone unpaired electron in Group 5.

Trend down a group

Decreases: atomic radius and shielding both increase down a group, and these effects outweigh the increase in nuclear charge — so the outer electron is easier to remove.

Successive ionisation energies

  • Each successive IE is always higher than the last — removing an electron from an increasingly positive ion requires more energy (stronger electrostatic attraction, and fewer electrons repelling each other).
  • Big jumps between successive IEs occur when the next electron removed comes from a shell closer to the nucleus (a new inner shell) — this is direct evidence for the existence of quantum shells.
  • Smaller step-changes within a shell's IEs can give evidence for subshells (e.g. a small jump when moving from removing p electrons to removing s electrons).
  • The position of a big jump tells you the number of outer-shell electrons, and therefore which group the element is in.
Definition
Define first ionisation energy.
The energy required to remove one electron from each atom in one mole of gaseous atoms, to form one mole of gaseous 1+ ions.
Explain
Why is there a drop in first IE from Mg to Al?
Al's outer electron is in a 3p subshell, which is higher in energy (and slightly further out) than the 3s subshell in Mg, so it's easier to remove despite Al having a greater nuclear charge.
Explain
Why does first IE decrease down a group?
Atomic radius and shielding both increase down the group, and these effects outweigh the increased nuclear charge, so the outer electron is less strongly attracted and easier to remove.
Evidence
What does a big jump in successive ionisation energies indicate?
That the next electron is being removed from a shell closer to the nucleus (a new, lower shell) — evidence for the existence of quantum shells.
Explain
Why is there a drop in first IE from P to S?
S has its first paired electron in a 3p orbital; the extra repulsion between paired electrons makes it easier to remove than P's unpaired outer electron.
1.5
Periodicity: Period 3 Physical Properties
▾

Atomic radius across Period 3

Decreases from Na to Ar. Nuclear charge increases while shielding stays roughly constant (same number of shells), so the outer electrons are pulled in more tightly — smaller radius.

First ionisation energy across Period 3

General increase from Na to Ar, with the same two anomalies as in 1.4 (dip at Al, dip at S) — same reasoning applies.

Melting point across Period 3

ElementNaMgAlSiPSClAr
StructureGiant metallicGiant covalentSimple molecular / monatomic
  • Na → Al: melting point rises. All are giant metallic lattices; going Na→Mg→Al, the number of delocalised electrons per atom increases (1, 2, 3) and ionic radius decreases, so metallic bonding gets stronger, requiring more energy to melt.
  • Si: huge peak — giant covalent (macromolecular) structure where every atom is joined by strong covalent bonds throughout the lattice; a large amount of energy is needed to break these bonds.
  • P, S, Cl, Ar: melting points are much lower and drop off — these exist as simple molecules (P₄, S₈, Cl₂) or monatomic (Ar), held together in the solid by weak London (van der Waals) forces between molecules. Melting only needs to overcome these weak intermolecular forces, not any covalent bonds.
  • Within this last group, S₈ > P₄ > Cl₂ > Ar because larger/more electrons in the molecule = stronger London forces (S₈ has the most electrons and the largest molecule of the four).
Common error: saying Si has a low melting point "because it's non-metal" — Si is a giant covalent structure with one of the highest melting points in the period. Don't confuse "non-metal" with "simple molecular."
Explain
Why does atomic radius decrease across Period 3?
Nuclear charge increases while shielding stays about the same (same shell), so outer electrons are pulled closer to the nucleus.
Explain
Why does melting point rise from Na to Al?
Number of delocalised electrons per atom increases (1→2→3) and ionic radius decreases, strengthening the metallic bonding, so more energy is needed to melt.
Explain
Why does silicon have the highest melting point in Period 3?
Silicon is a giant covalent (macromolecular) structure — every atom is bonded to its neighbours by strong covalent bonds throughout the lattice, requiring a large amount of energy to break.
Explain
Why do P, S, Cl and Ar have much lower melting points than Na–Si?
They exist as simple molecules or monatomic atoms held together only by weak London (van der Waals) forces, which take much less energy to overcome than breaking covalent or metallic bonds.
